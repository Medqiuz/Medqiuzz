Digestion and Absorption: Complete Guide

Digestion and Absorption

Complete Guide to Gastrointestinal Physiology, Enzymatic Breakdown, and Nutrient Uptake

Digestive System Overview

Primary Functions

The digestive system performs several critical functions essential for maintaining health and nutrition:

  • Reduces particle size of ingested food through mechanical and chemical processes
  • Facilitates absorption of micronutrients and trace elements
  • Establishes physical and immunologic barriers against pathogens and harmful substances
  • Processes and eliminates waste products from the body

Definition of Digestion

Digestion is the biochemical process that breaks down complex food molecules into simpler, absorbable units:

  • Carbohydrates (starch and sugars) → Monosaccharides (single sugar molecules)
  • Proteins → Amino acids
  • Fats (lipids) → Fatty acids and glycerol

Enzymes: The Biological Catalysts

Enzymes are specialized protein molecules that:

  • Accelerate chemical reactions without being consumed
  • Break specific bonds in food molecules
  • Function optimally under specific pH and temperature conditions
  • Are highly specific for their substrates (the molecules they act upon)

Anatomy of the Digestive System

Basic Structure

The gastrointestinal tract extends from the mouth to the anus and consists of several specialized layers:

Primary Components:

  • Epithelial lining facing the lumen - Forms a selective barrier against pathogens
  • Submucosal layer - Contains blood vessels, nerves, and glands
  • Muscularis - Smooth muscle layers responsible for motility
  • Sensory components - Taste and smell receptors that initiate digestive processes

Peristalsis: The Motility Mechanism

Peristalsis refers to the coordinated, wave-like muscular contractions that propel food along the entire gastrointestinal tract.

Illustration: Peristaltic contractions moving food mass through the GI tract

Muscle contractions create waves that push food forward while relaxing segments ahead

Key Point: Peristalsis is an involuntary process controlled by the enteric nervous system and occurs throughout the entire GI tract, from esophagus to rectum.

The Mouth and Oral Cavity

Mastication (Chewing)

Functions of Chewing:

  • Mechanically breaks down food into smaller particles
  • Increases surface area for enzymatic action
  • Mixes food with saliva to form a bolus for swallowing
  • Stimulates taste receptors to initiate digestive reflexes

Saliva and Salivary Glands

Salivary Production: Approximately 1.5 liters of saliva are produced daily by three pairs of major salivary glands:

  • Parotid glands - Largest, produce serous saliva rich in enzymes
  • Submandibular glands - Mixed serous and mucous secretion
  • Sublingual glands - Primarily mucous secretion

Components and Functions of Saliva:

  • Salivary amylase (ptyalin) - Begins starch digestion (inactivated in stomach acid)
  • Salivary lipase - Initiates fat digestion (minimal effect)
  • Mucus - Lubricates food for easier swallowing
  • Lysozyme - Antibacterial enzyme that kills bacteria
  • Water and electrolytes - Moisten food and maintain oral pH

The Tongue

Functions of the Tongue:

  • Taste perception via taste buds detecting five basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami (savory)
  • Mechanical manipulation of food during chewing
  • Secretion of lingual lipase - Enzyme that begins fat digestion
  • Formation of bolus for swallowing
  • Speech articulation

Swallowing and Esophagus

Swallowing (Deglutition)

The Swallowing Process:

  1. Oral Phase (Voluntary) - Tongue forms food into a bolus and pushes it toward the pharynx
  2. Pharyngeal Phase (Involuntary) - Soft palate elevates to close nasal passage; epiglottis covers larynx to prevent aspiration
  3. Esophageal Phase (Involuntary) - Peristalsis moves bolus through esophagus to stomach

Key Structure: The epiglottis is a flap-like structure that covers the trachea during swallowing, preventing food from entering the respiratory tract.

The Esophagus

Structure and Function:

  • Muscular tube approximately 25 cm long connecting pharynx to stomach
  • Transports food via peristalsis - coordinated wave-like muscular contractions
  • Contains two important sphincters:
    • Upper esophageal sphincter - Prevents air entry during breathing
    • Lower esophageal sphincter (LES) - Prevents gastric reflux into esophagus
  • Heartburn (acid reflux) occurs when LES malfunctions, allowing stomach acid to enter esophagus

The Stomach

Structure and Capacity

Key Features:

  • Storage capacity of approximately 4 cups (1 liter)
  • Regulated by two sphincters:
    • Lower esophageal sphincter (entry)
    • Pyloric sphincter (exit to duodenum)
  • Secretes 2,000-2,500 mL of gastric juice daily
  • Liquid meals exit in 1-2 hours, solid meals in 2-3 hours

Gastric Secretions

Components of Gastric Juice:

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl) - Creates acidic environment (pH 1-4)
  • Pepsinogen - Inactive precursor of pepsin (activated by HCl)
  • Gastric lipase - Begins fat digestion (especially short-chain triglycerides)
  • Mucus - Protective layer against self-digestion
  • Intrinsic factor - Essential for vitamin B₁₂ absorption
  • Gastrin - Hormone that stimulates acid secretion

Functions of Stomach Acid

Critical Roles of Gastric Acid:

  • Activates digestive enzymes (converts pepsinogen to pepsin)
  • Partially digests dietary proteins
  • Assists in calcium absorption by solubilizing calcium salts
  • Makes dietary minerals soluble for absorption
  • Kills bacteria and microorganisms ingested with food

Clinical Note: Achlorhydria (lack of stomach acid), gastrectomy, or GI dysfunction increases risk of bacterial overgrowth in the intestines due to reduced microbial killing.

Chyme Formation and Gastric Emptying

Chyme is the semi-fluid mass of partially digested food that exits the stomach into the small intestine.

Gastric Emptying Patterns:

  • Different nutrients exit at different rates (from fastest to slowest):
    1. Carbohydrates
    2. Proteins
    3. Fats
    4. Fibrous foods
  • The stomach absorbs very few nutrients directly (primarily some drugs and alcohol)
  • The mucus layer protects stomach lining from autodigestion

The Small Intestine

Structure and Divisions

Three Anatomical Segments (Total 5-7 meters):

  • Duodenum (0.5 meters) - Receives chyme from stomach, bile from liver, and pancreatic secretions
  • Jejunum (2-3 meters) - Primary site for nutrient absorption
  • Ileum (3-4 meters) - Completes absorption, especially of bile salts and vitamin B₁₂

Absorptive Surface Area

The small intestine has specialized structural adaptations that increase its surface area approximately 600-fold compared to a simple tube:

Surface Area Enhancements:

  1. Folds of Kerckring - Large circular folds in intestinal wall
  2. Villi - Finger-like projections (0.5-1.5 mm long) containing:
    • Absorptive cells (enterocytes)
    • Capillaries (for water-soluble nutrients)
    • Lacteals (lymphatic vessels for fat-soluble nutrients)
  3. Microvilli (Brush Border) - Microscopic projections on enterocytes forming the "brush border"

Cell Turnover: Intestinal epithelial cells have a rapid turnover rate of approximately 3-5 days, constantly renewing the absorptive surface.

Nutrient Absorption Mechanisms

Passive Diffusion

Driven by concentration gradient

  • No energy required
  • Examples: Water, small lipids, some minerals
  • Moves from high to low concentration

Active Absorption

Requires energy (ATP)

  • Against concentration gradient
  • Examples: Glucose, amino acids
  • Uses carrier proteins

Phagocytosis/Pinocytosis

Cell "drinking" or "eating"

  • Cell membrane engulfs substances
  • Examples: Immune substances in breast milk
  • For larger molecules

Absorption Pathways

After Digestion → Absorption → Transport:

  • Water-soluble nutrients (monosaccharides, amino acids, water-soluble vitamins) → Blood capillaries → Portal vein → Liver → General circulation
  • Fat-soluble nutrients (fatty acids, monoglycerides, fat-soluble vitamins) → Lymphatic lacteals → Thoracic duct → Left subclavian vein → General circulation (bypassing liver initially)

Liver Functions: The liver detoxifies absorbed substances and repackages nutrients for distribution to body tissues.

The Large Intestine

Structure and Function

Key Characteristics:

  • Length: Approximately 1.5 meters (5 feet)
  • No villi or digestive enzymes present
  • Little digestion occurs (except bacterial fermentation)
  • Primary functions:
    • Absorption of water and electrolytes
    • Formation and storage of feces
    • Bacterial fermentation of undigested material

Bacterial Action and Nutrient Production

Colonic Bacteria Functions:

  • Ferment indigestible carbohydrates (fiber) producing:
    • Gases (hydrogen, methane, carbon dioxide)
    • Short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, acetate, propionate)
  • Synthesize vitamins:
    • Vitamin K (essential for blood clotting)
    • Vitamin B₁₂, thiamin, riboflavin (in smaller amounts)
  • Compete with pathogens for resources
  • Metabolize bile acids

Rectum and Defecation

Defecation Process:

  1. Feces accumulate in rectum, causing distension
  2. Stretch receptors trigger defecation reflex
  3. Internal anal sphincter relaxes involuntarily
  4. External anal sphincter (under voluntary control) relaxes when appropriate
  5. Abdominal muscles contract to assist elimination

Accessory Digestive Organs

Salivary Glands

  • Produce 1.5L saliva daily
  • Contain salivary amylase (starch digestion)
  • Provide lubrication for swallowing
  • Contain antibacterial compounds

Pancreas

  • Endocrine function: Produces insulin and glucagon
  • Exocrine function: Produces digestive enzymes and bicarbonate
  • Bicarbonate neutralizes acidic chyme
  • Enzymes digest all macromolecules

Gallbladder

  • Stores and concentrates bile
  • Releases bile when needed for fat digestion
  • Contracts in response to cholecystokinin (CCK)
  • Bile emulsifies fats for digestion

Liver

  • Produces bile (1L daily)
  • Detoxifies absorbed substances
  • Metabolizes nutrients
  • Stores glycogen, vitamins, minerals
  • Synthesizes plasma proteins

Enterohepatic Circulation

Enterohepatic circulation refers to the recycling of bile salts between the liver and small intestine:

  1. Liver produces bile salts
  2. Bile salts aid fat digestion/absorption in small intestine
  3. 95% of bile salts reabsorbed in ileum
  4. Returned to liver via portal vein
  5. Recycled and resecreted

Carbohydrate Digestion Process

Starch Digestion Pathway:

  1. Mouth: Salivary amylase begins starch breakdown → dextrins
  2. Stomach: Amylase inactivated by acid (limited digestion)
  3. Small Intestine: Pancreatic amylase continues breakdown → maltose, maltotriose, dextrins
  4. Brush Border: Disaccharidases (maltase, sucrase, lactase) complete digestion → monosaccharides
  5. Absorption: Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose) absorbed via specific transporters

Fat Digestion and Absorption

Key Steps in Fat Processing:

  1. Emulsification: Bile salts break large fat globules into smaller droplets
  2. Enzymatic digestion: Pancreatic lipase breaks triglycerides into fatty acids and monoglycerides
  3. Micelle formation: Bile salts surround digestion products forming water-soluble micelles
  4. Absorption: Micelles transport lipids to enterocyte membrane
  5. Re-esterification: Triglycerides reformed inside enterocytes
  6. Chylomicron formation: Lipids packaged with proteins into chylomicrons
  7. Transport: Chylomicrons enter lymphatics (lacteals) → thoracic duct → bloodstream

Regulatory Systems

Norepinephrine Decreases motility, increases sphincter contractions Inhibits secretions, slows digestion (sympathetic response)
Acetylcholine Increases motility, relaxes sphincters Stimulates secretions, enhances digestion (parasympathetic)

Neuropeptide-Y Stimulates feeding behavior Increases appetite and food intake

Gastrointestinal Hormones

Gastrin

  • Source: Gastric mucosa, duodenum
  • Stimulus: Peptides, amino acids, gastric distension
  • Actions: Stimulates HCl and pepsinogen secretion; increases gastric motility; increases LES tone

Secretin

  • Source: Duodenal mucosa (S cells)
  • Stimulus: Acidic chyme in small intestine
  • Actions: Stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate secretion; stimulates biliary bicarbonate; inhibits gastric acid secretion

Cholecystokinin (CCK)

  • Source: Proximal small bowel (I cells)
  • Stimulus: Peptides, amino acids, fats, HCl
  • Actions: Stimulates pancreatic enzyme release; contracts gallbladder; inhibits gastric emptying; promotes satiety

GIP

  • Source: Small intestine (K cells)
  • Stimulus: Glucose, fats
  • Actions: Inhibits gastrin-stimulated acid secretion; stimulates insulin secretion (incretin effect)

Motilin

  • Source: Stomach, small and large intestine
  • Stimulus: Biliary and pancreatic secretions
  • Actions: Promotes gastric emptying; increases GI motility; regulates migrating motor complexes

Somatostatin

  • Source: Stomach, pancreas, upper small intestine
  • Stimulus: Acidity, protein/fat digestion products
  • Actions: Inhibits gastrin, motilin, pancreatic secretions; decreases GI motility

Key Concepts and Clinical Notes

Efficiency of Digestion and Absorption

Key Statistics:

  • 92-97% of dietary intake is absorbed under normal conditions
  • The small intestine is 7 meters long with extensive surface area modifications
  • Villi and microvilli increase absorptive surface 600-fold compared to a simple tube
  • Most digestion and absorption occurs in the small intestine

Refeeding After Starvation

Important Consideration: The gastrointestinal tract becomes compromised after prolonged starvation. When feeding resumes:

  • Cellular proliferation is stimulated
  • Normal gastrointestinal architecture and function are restored within days
  • Refeeding must be gradual to avoid complications like refeeding syndrome

Clinical Importance

Understanding GI Physiology is Essential:

  • Enables comprehension of pathophysiology of digestive disorders
  • Guides nutritional interventions for various medical conditions
  • Informs pharmacological approaches to GI diseases
  • Helps predict consequences of surgical interventions
  • Forms basis for diagnostic testing and interpretation
