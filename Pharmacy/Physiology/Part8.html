1. Gastrointestinal System Overview & Functions

Primary Function: To process food, extract nutrients, and eliminate waste through coordinated mechanical and chemical processes.

Four Major Activities of the GI Tract

  1. Motility - Propulsion of ingested food from mouth to rectum
    • Ingestion, mastication, deglutition
    • Peristalsis and segmentation
    • Defecation
  2. Secretion - Production of digestive juices
    • Saliva, gastric juice, pancreatic juice, bile
    • Mucus for lubrication and protection
    • Enzymes for chemical digestion
  3. Digestion - Breakdown of food into absorbable molecules
    • Mechanical: Chewing, churning, segmentation
    • Chemical: Enzymatic hydrolysis
    • Carbohydrates → Monosaccharides
    • Proteins → Amino acids
    • Fats → Fatty acids + Monoglycerides
  4. Absorption - Transport of nutrients from lumen to bloodstream
    • Small intestine: Primary site (90-95%)
    • Water and electrolyte balance
    • Vitamin and mineral uptake

Components of the Digestive System

Main Organs (Alimentary Canal)

  • Mouth - Ingestion, mechanical digestion, initial chemical digestion
  • Pharynx & Esophagus - Transportation to stomach
  • Stomach - Storage, mixing, protein digestion, intrinsic factor secretion
  • Small Intestine - Duodenum, Jejunum, Ileum - Main digestion and absorption
  • Large Intestine - Water absorption, feces formation, bacterial fermentation
  • Rectum & Anus - Feces storage and elimination

Accessory Organs

  • Teeth & Tongue - Mechanical digestion and taste
  • Salivary Glands - Saliva production (enzymes, mucus, ions)
  • Liver - Bile production, metabolic processing
  • Gallbladder - Bile storage and concentration
  • Pancreas - Digestive enzymes and bicarbonate secretion

Key Integration: The GI system functions as an integrated unit where motility, secretion, digestion, and absorption are precisely coordinated by neural and hormonal signals to optimize nutrient processing.

2. Gastrointestinal Tract Structure

Four-Layered Organization (From Lumen Outward)

1. Mucosa (Innermost Layer)

  • Epithelium - Variable along tract:
    • Stratified squamous in mouth, esophagus, anus (protection)
    • Simple columnar in stomach, intestines (absorption/secretion)
  • Lamina Propria - Loose connective tissue with:
    • Blood and lymphatic vessels
    • Nerve fibers
    • Lymphoid tissue (GALT - Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue)
  • Muscularis Mucosae - Thin smooth muscle layer
    • Creates local mucosal folding
    • Increases surface area
    • Aids in secretion and absorption

2. Submucosa

  • Dense connective tissue with larger blood vessels
  • Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus - part of ENS
  • Glands in some regions (esophageal, duodenal)
  • Lymphatic tissue

3. Muscularis Externa

  • Inner Circular Layer - Contraction narrows lumen
  • Outer Longitudinal Layer - Contraction shortens segment
  • Stomach has third oblique layer - Unique for mixing
  • Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus between layers
  • Coordinated contractions create peristalsis

4. Serosa/Adventitia

  • Serosa - Intraperitoneal organs (visceral peritoneum)
    • Simple squamous epithelium (mesothelium)
    • Connective tissue
  • Adventitia - Retroperitoneal organs
    • Connective tissue binding to surrounding structures

Gastric Structural Specialization

  • Three muscle layers (vs two elsewhere):
    • Outer longitudinal
    • Middle circular
    • Inner oblique (unique to stomach)
  • Thickness gradient: Proximal (thin, weak contractions) to distal (thick, strong contractions)
  • Gastric pits lead to glands with specialized cells

Important Distinction: The stomach wall thickness increases from the cardia (proximal) to the pylorus (distal), correlating with the strength of contractions needed for mixing versus propulsion.

Major Gastrointestinal Reflexes

Vagovagal Reflexes

Both afferent and efferent limbs in vagus nerve

  • Receptive relaxation - Stomach relaxes when food enters
  • Gastrocolic reflex - Stomach distension increases colon motility
  • Enterogastric reflex - Duodenal distension/inhibition slows gastric emptying

Other Important Reflexes

  • Defecation reflex - Rectal distension triggers desire to defecate
  • Rectosphincteric reflex - Internal anal sphincter relaxes when rectum fills
  • Ileogastric reflex - Ileal distension inhibits gastric motility
  • Intestino-intestinal reflex - Overdistension of one intestinal segment relaxes others

Reflex Pathway Example: Receptive Relaxation

1. Food enters stomach → Distension detected by mechanoreceptors

2. Afferent signal via vagus nerve to medulla

3. Efferent signal via vagus nerve back to stomach

4. VIP release → Smooth muscle relaxation → Stomach volume increases

Paracrine Regulators

Somatostatin

  • Source: D cells in stomach, pancreas, intestine
  • Stimuli: Acid, products of protein/fat digestion
  • Actions:
    • Inhibits gastrin, secretin, CCK, GIP release
    • Decreases gastric acid and pepsinogen secretion
    • Reduces pancreatic and biliary secretion
    • Slows GI motility and absorption
    • Decreases blood flow to GI tract

Histamine

  • Source: Enterochromaffin-like (ECL) cells in stomach
  • Stimuli: Gastrin, ACh
  • Action: Potent stimulator of gastric acid secretion via H₂ receptors on parietal cells
  • Clinical: H₂ receptor antagonists (ranitidine, famotidine) reduce acid secretion

Appetite and Satiety Regulation

Hypothalamic Centers

  • Ventromedial Nucleus (VMN): "Satiety center" - when stimulated, inhibits feeding
  • Lateral Hypothalamic Area (LHA): "Feeding center" - when stimulated, promotes feeding
  • Arcuate Nucleus: Integrates peripheral signals, contains:
    • Anorexigenic neurons: Release POMC/CART → decrease appetite
    • Orexigenic neurons: Release NPY/AgRP → increase appetite

5. Gastrointestinal Motility

Basic Principles

Definition: The contraction and relaxation of GI walls and sphincters that mix and propel contents.

Primary Functions:

  1. Propulsion: Move contents aborally (toward anus)
  2. Mixing: Combine contents with secretions
  3. Grinding: Reduce particle size
  4. Retention: Sphincters control passage between segments

Electrical Basis: Slow Waves and Interstitial Cells of Cajal

Slow Wave Characteristics

Definition: Oscillating depolarization and repolarization of smooth muscle membrane potential

Origin: Interstitial Cells of Cajal (ICC) - GI pacemaker cells

Frequency (varies by region):

  • Stomach: 3 waves/min
  • Duodenum: 12 waves/min (highest)
  • Ileum: 9 waves/min
  • Colon: 2-13 waves/min (variable)

Slow Wave Phases

  1. Depolarization Phase:
    • Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open
    • Ca²⁺ influx → inward current → depolarization
    • Membrane potential moves toward threshold (-40 mV)
  2. Plateau Phase:
    • If threshold reached, voltage-gated channels open
    • Action potentials occur at peak of slow wave
    • More action potentials = stronger contraction
  3. Repolarization Phase:
    • Voltage-gated K⁺ channels open
    • K⁺ efflux → outward current → repolarization
    • Returns to resting potential

Important: Slow waves themselves do NOT cause contraction. They set the maximum frequency at which contractions CAN occur. Contractions only happen when slow waves reach threshold and trigger action potentials.

Esophageal Motility

Pressure Dynamics

  • Intrathoracic pressure: -4 mmHg (subatmospheric)
  • Intraesophageal pressure: Equal to intrathoracic
  • Problem: Low pressure could suck air in (upper end) and allow reflux (lower end)
  • Solution: Sphincters remain closed except during swallowing

Swallowing Sequence

1. Oral Phase (voluntary): Bolus formation, tongue propulsion

2. Pharyngeal Phase (involuntary):
• Soft palate elevates (closes nasopharynx)
• Epiglottis covers larynx
• UES relaxes → bolus enters esophagus

3. Esophageal Phase (involuntary):
• Primary peristaltic wave (initiated by swallowing)
• LES relaxation (vagally-mediated VIP release)
• Receptive relaxation of proximal stomach
• Secondary peristalsis (if needed, triggered by local distension)

6. Secretory Functions of the GI Tract

General Principles

Secretory glands produce:

  1. Digestive enzymes (mouth to ileum)
  2. Mucus (mouth to anus) - lubrication and protection
  3. Water and electrolytes - create fluid environment
  4. Hormones - regulatory control

Gland Types

  • Goblet cells: Single mucus-secreting cells scattered throughout epithelium
  • Crypts of Lieberkühn: Simple tubular glands in small intestine
  • Oxyntic (gastric) glands: Deep tubular glands in stomach
  • Compound glands: Salivary glands, pancreas, liver (complex structure)

Mucus: The Universal Protectant

Mucus Composition and Properties

Composition: Water, electrolytes, glycoproteins (mucins)

Key Properties:

  • Adherent: Sticks to epithelium, forms continuous layer
  • Lubricating: Low resistance to slippage
  • Protective: Physical barrier against mechanical and chemical damage
  • Buffering: Contains bicarbonate that neutralizes acid
  • Resistant to digestion: Glycoproteins resist proteolytic enzymes
  • Amphoteric: Can buffer both acids and alkalis

Salivary Secretion

Saliva Composition and Formation

Daily volume: 800-1500 mL (average 1000 mL)

pH: 6.0-7.0 (optimal for α-amylase)

Key components:

  • α-amylase (ptyalin): Starch digestion
  • Lingual lipase: Fat digestion (minor role)
  • Mucin: Lubrication
  • Lysozyme: Antibacterial
  • IgA: Immune protection
  • Electrolytes: High K⁺ and HCO₃⁻, low Na⁺ and Cl⁻ vs plasma

Mechanism of Saliva Formation

Step 1 - Primary secretion (acini): Isotonic fluid similar to plasma

Step 2 - Ductal modification:
• Na⁺ actively reabsorbed
• K⁺ actively secreted
• HCO₃⁻ secreted (increases with flow rate)
• Cl⁻ passively follows Na⁺
• Water impermeable ducts (in resting state)

Result: Hypotonic final saliva (especially at low flow rates)

Regulation of Salivary Secretion

  • Primary control: Parasympathetic (dominant)
    • Stimulates copious, watery secretion
    • Via facial (VII) and glossopharyngeal (IX) nerves
  • Secondary: Sympathetic (minor role)
    • Stimulates small volume, mucus-rich secretion
    • Via cervical sympathetic chain
  • Stimuli: Taste (especially sour), smell, thought of food, conditioned reflexes
  • Kallikrein-bradykinin system: Local vasodilation during secretion

HCl Secretion Mechanism (Parietal Cell)

Step 1: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic anhydrase) → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

Step 2: H⁺ secreted into lumen via H⁺/K⁺ ATPase (proton pump)

Step 3: HCO₃⁻ exchanged for Cl⁻ at basolateral membrane (Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger)

Step 4: Cl⁻ diffuses into lumen through channels

Step 5: Water follows osmotically → isotonic HCl (160 mmol/L, pH 0.8)

Alkaline tide: HCO₃⁻ entering blood increases venous pH after meals

Regulation of Gastric Acid Secretion

Three Phases:
  1. Cephalic Phase (30% of response):
    • Sight, smell, taste, thought of food
    • Vagal stimulation → ACh release → direct parietal stimulation & gastrin release
  2. Gastric Phase (60% of response):
    • Food in stomach → distension & chemical stimuli
    • Distension → vagovagal reflex → ACh release
    • Peptides/amino acids → gastrin release
    • Gastrin & ACh → stimulate ECL cells → histamine release
    • Histamine (paracrine) → potent acid stimulation
  3. Intestinal Phase (10% of response):
    • Chyme in duodenum
    • Initially stimulatory (via gastrin release)
    • Then inhibitory (acid, fat, hypertonicity → secretin, CCK, GIP release)

Other Gastric Secretions

  • Pepsinogen:
    • Secreted by chief cells (and mucus cells)
    • Activated to pepsin by HCl (autoactivation possible)
    • Optimal pH 1.8-3.5 (inactive above pH 5)
    • Digests proteins to peptides
    • Regulated by vagal stimulation, local reflexes
  • Intrinsic Factor:
    • Secreted by parietal cells
    • Binds vitamin B₁₂ → protects from digestion
    • Essential for B₁₂ absorption in terminal ileum
    • Deficiency → pernicious anemia
  • Gastric Lipase:
    • Minor role in fat digestion
    • Optimal pH 3-6 (acid-stable)
    • Important in pancreatic insufficiency

Pancreatic Secretion

Pancreatic Juice Characteristics

Volume: 1.0-1.5 L/day

pH: 7.1-8.2 (alkaline)

Two Components:

  1. Aqueous (bicarbonate-rich):
    • Secreted by duct cells
    • Neutralizes gastric acid
    • Provides optimal pH for enzymes
    • Stimulated by secretin
  2. Enzymatic (enzyme-rich):
    • Secreted by acinar cells
    • Complete digestion of all macronutrients
    • Stimulated by CCK and vagal input

Pancreatic Enzymes

Protection Against Autodigestion

  1. Zymogen secretion: Proteases secreted as inactive precursors
  2. Enterokinase: Only on brush border (not in pancreas)
  3. Trypsin inhibitor: Inactivates any trypsin formed in pancreas
  4. Compartmentalization: Enzymes stored in zymogen granules
  5. pH control: Pancreatic juice is alkaline (not optimal for trypsin)

Pancreatitis: Occurs when protective mechanisms fail → premature activation → autodigestion.

Regulation of Pancreatic Secretion

Three Phases:
  1. Cephalic Phase (20%):
    • Sight, smell, taste of food
    • Vagal stimulation → enzyme-rich secretion
  2. Gastric Phase (10%):
    • Gastric distension → vagovagal reflex
    • Gastrin release → enzyme secretion
  3. Intestinal Phase (70%):
    • Chyme in duodenum
    • Secretin: Acid → bicarbonate-rich secretion
    • CCK: Fat/protein → enzyme-rich secretion
    • Vagal input potentiates both

Bile Composition

Volume: 600-1200 mL/day

pH: 7.8-8.6 (alkaline)

Major Components:

  • Water (97%): Vehicle for other components
  • Bile salts (0.7%): Cholic, chenodeoxycholic acids (conjugated with glycine/taurine)
  • Phospholipids (0.2%): Mainly lecithin (emulsification)
  • Cholesterol (0.06%): Excretory pathway
  • Bile pigments (0.2%): Bilirubin (breakdown product of heme)
  • Ions: Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Cl⁻, HCO₃⁻ (alkaline)

Bile Salt Synthesis and Recycling

Synthesis (Liver):

Step 1: Cholesterol → Primary bile acids (cholic & chenodeoxycholic) via 7α-hydroxylase (rate-limiting)

Step 2: Conjugation with glycine or taurine → increases solubility

Enterohepatic Circulation:

1. Bile secreted → duodenum → aid fat digestion/absorption

2. 94% reabsorbed: 50% passive (jejunum), 50% active (terminal ileum)

3. Portal blood → liver → resecreted

4. Recycling: 6-8 times/day, pool size: 3.5g, daily loss: 0.2-0.4g (feces)

5. 5-10% reach colon → bacterial modification → secondary bile acids (deoxycholic, lithocholic)

Functions of Bile Salts

  1. Emulsification: Break large fat globules into smaller droplets (increases surface area)
  2. Micelle formation: Transport fat digestion products to brush border
  3. Fat absorption: Essential for absorption of fatty acids, monoglycerides, cholesterol, fat-soluble vitamins
  4. Choleretic action: Stimulate bile flow (positive feedback)
  5. Cholesterol solubilization: Keep cholesterol dissolved in bile
  6. Laxative effect: Stimulate colonic motility

Gallbladder Functions

  1. Bile storage: Between meals (sphincter of Oddi closed)
  2. Concentration: 5-10× by absorbing water and electrolytes
  3. Acidification: HCO₃⁻ absorption → pH 7.0-7.4 (prevents Ca²⁺ precipitation)
  4. Mucus secretion: "White bile" protects mucosa
  5. Ejection: CCK-stimulated contraction + sphincter relaxation

Bilirubin Metabolism

Step 1: RBC breakdown (spleen) → Heme + Globin

Step 2: Heme → Biliverdin (green) → Unconjugated bilirubin (yellow)

Step 3: Unconjugated bilirubin + Albumin → Transport to liver

Step 4: Liver: Conjugation with glucuronic acid → Conjugated bilirubin (water-soluble)

Step 5: Secretion into bile → Intestine

Step 6: Intestine: Bacterial conversion → Urobilinogen

Step 7: Fate: 80% oxidized to stercobilin (brown feces), 20% reabsorbed → kidney → urobilin (yellow urine)

Intestinal Secretions

Small Intestine Secretions

  • Volume: ~1800 mL/day
  • pH: 7.5-8.0 (alkaline)
  • Sources:
    • Brunner's glands (duodenum): Alkaline mucus (protects from acid)
    • Crypts of Lieberkühn: Watery fluid (provides vehicle for digestion/absorption)
    • Enterocytes (brush border): Enzymes (sucrase, maltase, lactase, peptidases)
  • Regulation: Mainly local (tactile, chemical stimuli), ENS, hormones

Large Intestine Secretions

  • Volume: Small (mostly mucus)
  • Composition: Mucus (with HCO₃⁻), no digestive enzymes
  • Functions:
    • Protection against mechanical injury
    • Lubrication for fecal passage
    • Adherence of fecal matter
    • Neutralization of acids from bacterial fermentation
  • Regulation: Local tactile stimuli, parasympathetic stimulation

7. Digestion Processes

General Principles

Digestion: The process of breaking down complex food molecules into absorbable units through mechanical and chemical means.

End Products:

  • Carbohydrates → Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose)
  • Proteins → Amino acids, dipeptides, tripeptides
  • Fats → Fatty acids, monoglycerides
  • Nucleic acids → Nucleotides, nucleosides, bases

Carbohydrate Digestion

Complete Starch Digestion Pathway

1. Mouth (Minor Digestion):

• Salivary α-amylase (ptyalin)
• Acts on cooked starch (α-1,4 glycosidic bonds only)
• Produces: Maltose, maltotriose, α-limit dextrins
• Inactivated in stomach when pH < 4.5

2. Stomach (No Carbohydrate Digestion):

• Acid environment inactivates salivary amylase

3. Small Intestine (Main Digestion Site):

• Pancreatic α-amylase (more potent than salivary)
• Continues starch breakdown → maltose, maltotriose, α-limit dextrins

4. Brush Border (Final Digestion):

• Maltase: Maltose → 2 glucose
• Sucrase: Sucrose → glucose + fructose
• Lactase: Lactose → glucose + galactose
• α-dextrinase (isomaltase): α-limit dextrins → glucose (cleaves α-1,6 bonds)

5. Final Products for Absorption:

• Glucose (80%), Galactose (10%), Fructose (10%)

Protein Digestion

Complete Protein Digestion Pathway

1. Stomach (Initial Digestion):

• HCl denatures proteins (unfolds tertiary structure)
• Pepsin (from pepsinogen activated by HCl)
• Cleaves peptide bonds adjacent to aromatic amino acids
• Produces: Large polypeptides, some smaller peptides

2. Small Intestine (Main Digestion):

Pancreatic Proteases (all secreted as zymogens):
• Trypsin (from trypsinogen via enterokinase): Cleaves after basic amino acids (Arg, Lys)
• Chymotrypsin (from chymotrypsinogen via trypsin): Cleaves after aromatic amino acids (Phe, Trp, Tyr)
• Elastase (from proelastase via trypsin): Cleaves after small neutral amino acids
• Carboxypeptidase A & B (from procarboxypeptidase via trypsin): Remove C-terminal amino acids

3. Brush Border (Final Digestion):

• Aminopeptidases: Remove N-terminal amino acids
• Dipeptidases, Tripeptidases: Hydrolyze small peptides

4. Final Products for Absorption:

• Amino acids (70%), Dipeptides (20%), Tripeptides (10%)

Activation Cascade: Enterokinase (brush border) activates trypsinogen → trypsin → trypsin activates all other pancreatic proteases. This ensures activation only occurs in the duodenum, preventing pancreatic autodigestion.

Fat Digestion

Complete Fat Digestion Pathway

1. Mouth & Stomach (Minor Digestion):

• Lingual lipase (mouth) & Gastric lipase (stomach)
• Act on short/medium-chain triglycerides
• Acid-stable (optimal pH 3-6)
• Minor contribution in adults, important in infants/pancreatic insufficiency

2. Small Intestine (Main Digestion Site):

Step 1 - Emulsification:
• Bile salts reduce surface tension
• Mechanical mixing (peristalsis)
• Large fat globules → small droplets (increases surface area 1000×)

Step 2 - Enzymatic Digestion:
• Pancreatic lipase-colipase complex (main enzyme)
• Hydrolyzes triglycerides at positions 1 & 3 → 2-monoglycerides + 2 fatty acids
• Cholesterol esterase: Cholesterol esters → cholesterol + fatty acids
• Phospholipase A2 (activated by trypsin): Phospholipids → lyso-phospholipids + fatty acids

Step 3 - Micelle Formation:
• Bile salts + phospholipids form micelles
• Hydrophobic core contains digestion products
• Hydrophilic exterior allows water solubility
• Transport to brush border for absorption

3. Intracellular Processing (Enterocytes):

• Re-esterification: Fatty acids + monoglycerides → triglycerides
• + Cholesterol, phospholipids, apoproteins → Chylomicrons
• Exocytosis → lymphatics (lacteals) → thoracic duct → systemic circulation

Critical Role of Bile Salts: Without bile salts (liver disease, bile duct obstruction, ileal resection), fat digestion is incomplete → steatorrhea (fatty stools), fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies.

Nucleic Acid Digestion

DNA/RNA Digestion Pathway

  1. Pancreatic nucleases:
    • Ribonuclease: RNA → oligonucleotides
    • Deoxyribonuclease: DNA → oligonucleotides
  2. Brush border enzymes:
    • Nucleotidases: Remove phosphate → nucleosides
    • Nucleosidases: Cleave sugar-base bond
  3. Final products: Purine/pyrimidine bases, pentose sugars, phosphate
  4. Absorption: Mostly as nucleosides via specific carriers

Site-Specific Absorption

Absorption Sites Along GI Tract

Duodenum: Iron, calcium, magnesium, zinc, copper, folate

Jejunum (PRIMARY SITE): Most nutrients, vitamins, electrolytes

Ileum: Vitamin B₁₂, bile salts, remaining nutrients

Carbohydrate Absorption

Monosaccharide Transport Mechanisms

1. Glucose & Galactose (SGLT1 - Secondary Active):

• Apical membrane: SGLT1 (Na⁺-glucose cotransporter)
• 2 Na⁺ : 1 glucose stoichiometry
• Na⁺ gradient (maintained by basolateral Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase) drives uptake
• Basolateral exit: GLUT2 (facilitated diffusion)

2. Fructose (GLUT5 - Facilitated Diffusion):

• Apical: GLUT5 (specific for fructose)
• No energy requirement, slower than glucose
• Some conversion to glucose inside enterocyte
• Basolateral exit: GLUT2 (with glucose/galactose)

3. Pentose Sugars (ribose, xylose):

• Passive diffusion (slow)

Fat Absorption

Step-by-Step Fat Absorption

Step 1 - Micelle Delivery:
• Mixed micelles (bile salts + phospholipids + fat digestion products) diffuse through unstirred water layer
• Deliver lipids to brush border membrane

Step 2 - Cellular Uptake:
• Fatty acids, monoglycerides, cholesterol diffuse across apical membrane
• Some evidence for specific transporters (FATP4, CD36)

Step 3 - Intracellular Processing:
• Fatty acid binding protein (FABP) transports to smooth ER
• Re-esterification: Fatty acids + monoglycerides → triglycerides (MGAT, DGAT enzymes)
• Cholesterol re-esterification (ACAT enzyme)
• Phospholipid synthesis

Step 4 - Chylomicron Assembly:
• Triglycerides + cholesterol esters + phospholipids + apoproteins (B-48, A-I, A-IV)
• In Golgi apparatus

Step 5 - Exocytosis & Transport:
• Chylomicrons exocytosed at basolateral membrane
• Enter lymphatics (lacteals) → thoracic duct → left subclavian vein → systemic circulation
• Bypass liver initially (unlike other nutrients)

Medium-Chain Triglycerides (MCTs): Unlike long-chain fats, MCTs (6-12 carbons) are more water-soluble, can be absorbed without micelles, enter portal blood (not lymph), and don't require chylomicron formation. Used in malabsorption conditions.

Vitamin Absorption

Fat-Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K)

  • Requirement: Bile salts for micelle formation
  • Mechanism: Incorporated into mixed micelles → passive diffusion
  • Intracellular: Incorporated into chylomicrons
  • Transport: Lymphatics → systemic circulation
  • Deficiency: Occurs in fat malabsorption (steatorrhea)

Vitamin B₁₂ Absorption (Complex Pathway)

Step 1 - Gastric Phase:
• Dietary B₁₂ bound to proteins → released by pepsin/HCl
• Binds R-protein (haptocorrin) from saliva

Step 2 - Duodenal Phase:
• Pancreatic proteases degrade R-protein
• B₁₂ released, binds intrinsic factor (IF from parietal cells)

Step 3 - Ileal Phase:
• IF-B₁₂ complex binds cubilin receptor on ileal brush border
• Receptor-mediated endocytosis

Step 4 - Transport:
• Released into portal blood bound to transcobalamin II
• Delivery to tissues

Deficiency causes: Pernicious anemia (autoimmune vs parietal cells/IF), gastrectomy, ileal resection, bacterial overgrowth

Mineral Absorption

Major Mineral Absorption Mechanisms

Calcium:
  • Active transport (duodenum, jejunum):
    • TRPV6 (apical Ca²⁺ channel)
    • Calbindin-D9k (intracellular transport)
    • PMCA1b (basolateral Ca²⁺ ATPase)
    • Vitamin D upregulates TRPV6 and calbindin
  • Passive paracellular (throughout intestine): Concentration-dependent
Iron:
  • Heme iron (meat):
    • HCP1 transporter → enterocyte
    • Heme oxygenase releases Fe²⁺
  • Non-heme iron (Fe³⁺):
    • Reduced to Fe²⁺ by DcytB (duodenal cytochrome B)
    • DMT1 transporter (apical, H⁺-coupled)
  • Intracellular: Stored as ferritin or exported via ferroportin
  • Basolateral export: Ferroportin + hephaestin (oxidizes to Fe³⁺ for transferrin binding)
  • Regulation: Hepcidin (liver hormone) inhibits ferroportin → decreases absorption
Sodium:
  • Active (throughout): Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase (basolateral) creates gradient
  • Coupled absorption: With nutrients (glucose, amino acids), Cl⁻, bile acids
  • Passive paracellular: Follows osmotic gradients

Water Absorption Mechanism

Osmosis: Water follows osmotic gradients created by active solute absorption

Routes:

  1. Transcellular: Through aquaporins (water channels) in enterocytes
  2. Paracellular: Between cells through tight junctions (depends on tightness)

Coupling with Solute Absorption:

  • Na⁺ absorption creates osmotic gradient → water follows
  • Nutrient absorption (glucose, amino acids) enhances Na⁺ and water absorption
  • This is exploited in oral rehydration therapy (glucose + NaCl)

9. System Integration and Coordination

Meal Processing Timeline

Postprandial Sequence of Events

0-1 Hour (Cephalic & Early Gastric Phase):
  • Sight/smell/taste of food → vagal stimulation
  • Increased salivary and gastric secretion
  • Receptive relaxation of stomach
  • Initial gastric mixing
1-3 Hours (Gastric & Early Intestinal Phase):
  • Gastric secretion peaks (histamine-mediated)
  • Protein digestion begins (pepsin)
  • Carbohydrates begin leaving stomach
  • Chyme enters duodenum → CCK, secretin release
  • Gallbladder contraction, pancreatic secretion
3-6 Hours (Intestinal Phase):
  • Main digestion and absorption in small intestine
  • Fat digestion/absorption (requires bile, slower)
  • Nutrient absorption stimulates insulin release (incretin effect)
  • Ileal brake slows gastric emptying
6+ Hours (Interdigestive Phase):
  • Migrating motor complexes clear residual material
  • Motilin coordinates housekeeping waves
  • Colon processes residue, absorbs water

Neural-Hormonal Integration

Integrated Response to a Meal

Sensory Input (sight, smell, taste) → Vagal Activation →

• Gastric acid secretion (direct + via gastrin)

• Pancreatic enzyme secretion

• Gallbladder contraction (minor)

• Receptive relaxation

Gastric Phase: Distension + peptides → Gastrin release →

• Acid secretion (direct + via histamine)

• Gastric motility

Intestinal Phase: Chyme components →

• Acid → Secretin → Bicarbonate secretion

• Fat/Protein → CCK → Enzymes, gallbladder contraction

• Nutrients → GIP → Insulin release

• All → Enterogastric reflex → Slowed gastric emptying

Interorgan Communication

Key Communication Pathways

1. Gastropancreatic Reflex:
  • Gastric distension → vagovagal reflex → pancreatic secretion
  • Ensures pancreatic enzymes ready when chyme arrives
2. Enteroinsular Axis:
  • Nutrients in intestine → GIP, GLP-1 release → insulin secretion
  • Explains why oral glucose elicits greater insulin response than IV glucose (incretin effect)
3. Enterohepatic Circulation:
  • Bile salts reabsorbed in ileum → portal blood → liver → resecretion
  • Conserves bile salt pool (only 5-10% lost daily)
4. Gastrocolic Reflex:
  • Gastric distension → increased colonic motility
  • Often produces urge to defecate after meals
5. Ileal Brake:
  • Nutrients in ileum → PYY, GLP-1 release → slowed gastric emptying
  • Prevents overloading of digestion/absorption capacity

Adaptive Responses

System Adaptation to Different Meals

High-Carbohydrate Meal:
  • Rapid gastric emptying (carbohydrates empty fastest)
  • Pancreatic amylase secretion adequate for starch load
  • Brush border disaccharidases not rate-limiting
  • Strong incretin effect (GIP from glucose)
High-Fat Meal:
  • Slow gastric emptying (fat stimulates CCK)
  • Strong gallbladder contraction (CCK-mediated)
  • Pancreatic lipase secretion increased
  • Micelle formation critical for absorption
  • Satiety signals prolonged (CCK, PYY)
High-Protein Meal:
  • Moderate gastric emptying rate
  • Strong gastrin stimulation (from amino acids)
  • Increased pancreatic protease secretion
  • Active amino acid and peptide transporters not saturated

System Efficiency: The GI tract processes approximately 30 tons of food and 50,000 liters of fluid in an average lifetime, with 92-97% efficiency in nutrient extraction, demonstrating remarkable integration of its component functions.

Peptic Ulcer Disease

Pathophysiology

Imbalance between aggressive and defensive factors:

Aggressive Factors:
  • HCl acid
  • Pepsin
  • H. pylori infection (80% of ulcers)
  • NSAIDs (inhibit prostaglandin synthesis)
Defensive Factors:
  • Mucus-bicarbonate barrier
  • Mucosal blood flow
  • Epithelial cell renewal
  • Prostaglandins (stimulate mucus and bicarbonate)

H. pylori Mechanism

1. Flagella allow movement through mucus layer

2. Urease converts urea → NH₃ + CO₂ (neutralizes local acid)

3. Adhesins bind to epithelial cells

4. Toxins (VacA, CagA) cause cell damage

5. Inflammation → increased gastrin → increased acid

6. Combined acid increase + mucosal damage → ulcer

Gallstone Disease

Types and Pathogenesis

Cholesterol Stones (80%):
  • Requirements:
    1. Cholesterol supersaturation in bile
    2. Nucleation (mucin, calcium salts as nidus)
    3. Impaired gallbladder emptying (stasis)
  • Risk factors: Female, Forty, Fertile, Fat, Fair (5 F's), rapid weight loss, TPN, ileal disease
Pigment Stones (20%):
  • Black stones: Hemolytic disorders, cirrhosis (increased unconjugated bilirubin)
  • Brown stones: Bacterial infection (β-glucuronidase deconjugates bilirubin)

Consequences of Gallstones

  • Asymptomatic: Majority (80%)
  • Biliary colic: Stone transiently obstructs cystic duct
  • Acute cholecystitis: Impacted stone → inflammation
  • Choledocholithiasis: Stone in common bile duct → jaundice, pancreatitis
  • Gallstone ileus: Fistula to intestine → mechanical obstruction

Liver Disease Manifestations

Consequences of Liver Dysfunction

1. Jaundice:
  • Pre-hepatic: Excess bilirubin production (hemolysis)
  • Hepatic: Impaired conjugation/excretion (hepatitis, cirrhosis)
  • Post-hepatic: Biliary obstruction (stones, tumor)
2. Portal Hypertension:
  • Increased resistance to portal flow
  • Consequences: Varices (esophageal, gastric), ascites, splenomegaly
3. Synthetic Failure:
  • Coagulopathy: Reduced clotting factors (increased PT/INR)
  • Hypoalbuminemia: Edema, ascites
4. Detoxification Failure:
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: Ammonia accumulation → neuropsychiatric symptoms
  • Drug toxicity: Reduced metabolism

Pancreatitis

Pathogenesis and Consequences

Mechanisms:
  1. Obstructive: Gallstone impacts pancreatic duct → increased pressure
  2. Toxic: Alcohol → protein plugs → duct obstruction
  3. Metabolic: Hypertriglyceridemia (>1000 mg/dL)
  4. Common pathway: Premature activation of trypsin → autodigestion
Local Complications:
  • Pancreatic necrosis, pseudocyst, abscess
  • Fluid collections (lesser sac, retroperitoneum)
Systemic Complications:
  • ARDS (acute respiratory distress syndrome)
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Shock (fluid sequestration, vasodilation)
  • Hypocalcemia (saponification of fat)

Refeeding Syndrome

Pathophysiology After Starvation

Starvation adaptations:

  • Catabolic state → using fat/protein for energy
  • Electrolyte depletion (K⁺, Mg²⁺, PO₄³⁻)
  • Reduced insulin secretion
  • GI tract atrophy (villous shortening, enzyme reduction)

Refeeding response:

  • Carbohydrate intake → insulin surge
  • Electrolyte shifts into cells (hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia)
  • Fluid retention (Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase reactivation)
  • Increased metabolic demand (thiamine especially)

Consequences: Cardiac arrhythmias, respiratory failure, neurological symptoms, fluid overload

Prevention: Gradual refeeding, electrolyte monitoring/replacement, thiamine administration

Clinical Integration: Understanding GI physiology allows prediction of which deficiencies will occur in specific diseases and guides targeted therapy (e.g., pancreatic enzyme replacement in cystic fibrosis, bile acid sequestrants in bile acid diarrhea, lactase supplements in lactose intolerance).
