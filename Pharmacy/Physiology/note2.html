A Comprehensive Guide to Hormone Physiology

Hormone Basics

What Is a Hormone?

Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands that are released into the bloodstream to reach distant target cells. They ensure precise communication across the body, coordinating various physiological processes.

These chemical messengers can be classified into three main types:

  • Peptides and proteins - Water-soluble hormones that cannot cross cell membranes
  • Steroids - Lipid-soluble hormones derived from cholesterol
  • Amines - Including catecholamines and thyroid hormones with variable solubility

Receptor-Mediated Specificity

Each hormone binds only to its specific receptor, much like a key fitting into a lock. This precise binding triggers defined cellular responses that maintain internal balance (homeostasis).

There are two main types of hormone receptors:

  • Membrane receptors - Located on the cell surface, using second messengers like cAMP or IP3
  • Nuclear receptors - Located inside cells, directly affecting gene transcription

Homeostasis Guardians

Hormones maintain homeostasis by adjusting metabolism, growth, and reproduction. They keep critical parameters like temperature, glucose levels, and electrolyte concentrations within narrow, life-supporting ranges.

This regulatory function ensures our bodies can adapt to changing internal and external environments while maintaining stability.

Chemical Classes of Hormones

Peptide and Protein Hormones

These hydrophilic molecules cannot cross cell membranes and act via surface receptors. They trigger intracellular second messengers like cAMP or IP3 to relay their signals throughout the cell.

Steroid Hormones

Derived from cholesterol, these lipophilic hormones can penetrate cell membranes and bind to nuclear receptors. They directly alter gene transcription and protein synthesis, with effects that can last from hours to days.

Amine Hormones

This category includes catecholamines and thyroid hormones. They exhibit variable solubility, with some acting on surface receptors and others entering cells to affect gene expression.

Receptor Types and Signaling

Membrane Receptors

These receptors use second messengers such as cAMP, IP3, or kinase cascades to rapidly transmit hormonal signals from the cell surface to intracellular targets. This allows for quick cellular responses to hormonal signals.

Nuclear Receptors

Lipid-soluble hormones bind these receptors inside the cell. The hormone-receptor complex then attaches to specific DNA response elements, modulating gene transcription over hours to days, leading to longer-lasting effects.

Hormone Transport and Half-Life

Protein Binding Extends Duration

Steroids and thyroid hormones bind to plasma proteins, which prolongs their half-life and buffers against sudden fluctuations in hormone levels. This creates a reservoir of hormone that can be released as needed.

Free Peptides Are Short-Lived

Peptide hormones circulate unbound and are rapidly degraded by enzymes in the blood and tissues. This results in short half-lives (minutes), allowing for quick adjustments in physiological responses.

Clearance Mechanisms

The liver and kidneys metabolize and excrete hormones. The efficiency of these clearance mechanisms determines how long each hormone remains active in the bloodstream.

Endocrine vs Neural Control

Speed and Precision of Neural Signals

Neurons transmit signals within milliseconds, offering spatial precision and rapid reversibility. This makes neural control ideal for immediate reflexes and acute responses to environmental changes.

Endocrine Breadth and Persistence

Hormones act over seconds to hours, broadcast systemically throughout the body via the bloodstream. They persist until metabolized, making them suited for widespread, long-lasting regulatory effects.

Pulsatile and Circadian Secretion

Pulsatile Patterns Prevent Desensitization

Many hormones are released in rhythmic bursts rather than continuously. This pattern maintains receptor sensitivity and ensures a wide dynamic range for physiological responses, preventing receptor down-regulation.

Circadian Rhythms Align Metabolism

Clock genes entrain hormones like cortisol and melatonin, synchronizing metabolic processes with daily sleep-wake and feeding cycles. This alignment optimizes energy utilization throughout the day.

Assessing Hormone Action

Plasma Assays Measure Concentration

Blood tests quantify hormone levels, but concentration does not always equate to biological activity. Interpretation requires understanding of the clinical context, binding proteins, and target tissue responsiveness.

Dynamic Testing Evaluates Reserve

Stimulation or suppression tests probe glandular capacity and feedback integrity. These tests can reveal subclinical dysfunction before basal hormone levels change significantly.

Integrated Biomarkers Reflect Activity

Target-tissue responses, genomic signatures, and metabolic fluxes provide a holistic view of hormone effectiveness in living organisms, beyond simple concentration measurements.

Endocrine Disruptors Overview

Chemical Mimics Block Hormones

Environmental substances like bisphenols and pesticides can bind hormone receptors, either blocking or exaggerating natural signals. This interference can derail normal development and physiological processes.

Transgenerational Epigenetic Effects

Early-life exposure to endocrine disruptors can modify gene expression patterns that persist across generations. This highlights critical windows of vulnerability during development.

Hormone Synthesis Pathways

Peptide Route

Gene transcription → mRNA → ribosomal preprohormone → signal peptide cleaved in ER → prohormone packaged into secretory granules → mature hormone released upon stimulation.

Steroid Route

Cholesterol imported or synthesized → side-chain cleavage in mitochondria → sequential enzymatic modifications in smooth ER and cytosol → final steroid hormone product.

Amine Route

Tyrosine uptake → hydroxylation to L-DOPA → decarboxylation to dopamine → β-hydroxylation to norepinephrine → N-methylation to epinephrine (in adrenal medulla).

Autocrine vs Paracrine vs Endocrine

Autocrine Signaling

A cell secretes a hormone that binds to receptors on itself, creating self-regulating feedback loops for rapid local adjustments in cellular activity.

Paracrine Signaling

Secreted molecules diffuse through interstitial fluid to neighboring cells, coordinating tissue responses without entering the bloodstream.

Endocrine Signaling

Hormones travel via blood to distant organs, allowing integrated systemic control of growth, metabolism, and reproduction throughout the body.

Negative Feedback Loops

Physiological Brake System

Rising hormone levels directly or indirectly inhibit further release, preventing extreme fluctuations and maintaining set-point stability. This is the most common regulatory mechanism in endocrine systems.

Insulin Example

Post-prandial insulin lowers blood glucose, and the resulting hypoglycemia suppresses β-cell secretion, protecting against excessive drops in blood sugar levels.

Positive Feedback Example

Accelerating Cascade

Output stimulates even more output until an endpoint is reached, creating rapid, self-limiting surges critical for specific biological events like childbirth and ovulation.

Ovulatory Estradiol Surge

Mid-cycle estradiol peaks stimulate hypothalamic GnRH and pituitary LH release, culminating in the LH surge that triggers ovulation - a classic example of positive feedback.

Receptor Down-Regulation

Desensitization Mechanism

Persistent high hormone concentrations reduce receptor number or affinity, blunting cellular responsiveness and protecting against overstimulation.

Clinical Relevance

This phenomenon explains tolerance to therapeutic agents such as β-agonists in asthma or levodopa in Parkinson disease, requiring dose adjustments or drug holidays.

Receptor Up-Regulation

Sensitization Strategy

Low hormone levels increase receptor synthesis or binding affinity, amplifying tissue sensitivity when signal availability is limited.

Prolactin During Pregnancy

An example where receptor up-regulation prepares mammary tissue for lactation in response to increasing prolactin levels during pregnancy.

Second Messenger Systems

cAMP Pathway

Hormone binds G-protein-coupled receptor → GDP-GTP exchange on Gαs subunit → GTP-Gαs activates adenylyl cyclase → converts ATP to cyclic AMP → cAMP activates protein kinase A → phosphorylation of target proteins → signal terminated by phosphodiesterase and intrinsic GTPase activity.

IP3-Calcium Cascade

Gq-coupled receptors activate phospholipase C → cleaves PIP2 into IP3 and DAG → IP3 diffuses to ER and opens Ca2+ channels → cytosolic Ca2+ increases → Ca2+ binds DAG to activate protein kinase C → Ca2+-calmodulin complexes activate additional kinases and phosphatases.

Catalytic Receptors

Guanylyl cyclase receptors activated by ANP and nitric oxide → convert GTP to cGMP → activates protein kinase G → phosphorylates targets for vasodilation and natriuresis. Tyrosine kinase receptors undergo autophosphorylation upon ligand binding → create docking sites for adaptor proteins → activate downstream signaling cascades.

Steroid Hormone Action

Lipid-soluble steroids pass plasma and nuclear membranes unaided → reach cytosolic or nuclear receptors → hormone-receptor complex binds to specific DNA sequences → alters gene transcription → new protein synthesis → cellular response. The time lag between hormone binding and observable effect reflects the time needed for protein synthesis.

Pituitary Gland

Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

Portal Vascular Link

Hypophyseal portal veins carry releasing hormones from median eminence capillaries directly to anterior pituitary cells, ensuring high local concentrations without systemic dilution.

Releasing Hormones

Hypothalamic neurons synthesize and release specific hormones that control anterior pituitary function, including TRH, CRH, GnRH, GHRH, and somatostatin.

Rapid Response

This direct vascular connection allows for swift endocrine adjustments in response to changing physiological demands.

Hypothalamic Structure Overview

Integration Hub for Homeostasis

The hypothalamus collects neural, metabolic, and hormonal inputs, processing them to orchestrate responses that maintain energy balance, temperature, and stress adaptation.

Specialized Neuron Populations

Parvocellular neurons release hypophyseotropic hormones into portal blood, while magnocellular neurons project to the posterior pituitary, exemplifying direct neuroendocrine output.

Portal Circulation Anatomy

Private Vascular Highway

Primary capillaries in the median eminence capture releasing hormones, and portal veins deliver them undiluted to the anterior pituitary, bypassing systemic circulation.

Rapid Concentrated Delivery

This arrangement ensures that minute quantities of hypothalamic factors reach their target cells within seconds, enabling precise control of pituitary function.

Anterior Pituitary Hormones

Tropic Hormones Control Peripheral Glands

ACTH, TSH, LH, and FSH stimulate adrenal, thyroid, and gonadal hormone synthesis, forming the backbone of classical endocrine axes.

Growth and Lactation Regulators

GH promotes somatic growth and metabolic balance, while prolactin drives mammary development and milk production, each under dual hypothalamic control.

Secretory Patterns Matter

These peptide hormones are released in pulses, preventing receptor desensitization and allowing nuanced responses to changing physiological demands.

Posterior Pituitary Hormones

Hormones Made in Hypothalamus

Oxytocin and vasopressin are synthesized in supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei, transported down axons complexed with neurophysins for storage in nerve terminals.

Neuroendocrine Reflex Release

Suckling, osmotic shifts, or blood pressure changes trigger calcium-dependent exocytosis at nerve terminals, releasing hormones directly into blood.

CRH-ACTH-Adrenal Cascade

Sequential Hormone Activation

CRH stimulates POMC processing to ACTH in corticotropes; ACTH binds MC2 receptors on adrenal cells, activating cAMP-PKA to synthesize cortisol.

Ultradian and Circadian Feedback

Cortisol feeds back at hypothalamic and pituitary levels, shaping both pulsatile bursts and daily rhythm entrained to sleep-wake cycles.

TRH-TSH-Thyroid Axis

TRH Drives TSH Synthesis

Paraventricular neurons release TRH, which binds pituitary thyrotropes, stimulating TSH-beta gene transcription and glycoprotein hormone assembly.

T3 Feedback Closes the Loop

Circulating T3 enters pituitary and hypothalamus, binding thyroid receptors to repress TRH and TSH, maintaining thyroid hormone homeostasis.

GnRH Pulse Generator

Rhythmic Neuronal Firing

Hypothalamic GnRH neurons discharge every 60–90 minutes, releasing pulses into portal blood, a pattern essential for maintaining gonadotrope sensitivity.

Frequency Encodes Differential Output

Faster pulses favor LH, slower pulses favor FSH, enabling fine control over gametogenesis and steroidogenesis throughout reproductive life.

Growth Hormone Regulation

Dual Hypothalamic Control

GHRH stimulates GH gene expression and secretion, while somatostatin inhibits it, creating a push-pull system responsive to metabolic state.

Ghrelin Amplifies Pulses

The gastric hormone ghrelin binds pituitary receptors, potentiating GHRH effects and synchronizing GH surges with hunger and meal anticipation.

IGF-1 Negative Feedback

Hepatic IGF-1 feeds back on pituitary and hypothalamus, limiting GH output and linking somatic growth to nutritional availability.

Prolactin Control Logic

Dopamine Tonically Inhibits

Arcuate neurons release dopamine onto lactotrope D2 receptors, suppressing PRL gene transcription and secretion under basal conditions.

Suckling Triggers Disinhibition

Sensory input from nipple reduces dopaminergic tone, while estrogen and TRH further stimulate PRL, enabling lactation and maternal behavior.

Clinical Axis Disorders

Hyperfunction from Adenomas

Pituitary tumors can hyper-secrete ACTH, GH, or PRL, causing Cushing disease, acromegaly, or galactorrhea-amenorrhea syndromes requiring targeted therapy.

Hypofunction from Infarction

Sheehan postpartum infarction, trauma, or infiltrative diseases destroy hormone-producing cells, necessitating lifelong replacement and dynamic monitoring.

Growth Hormone Overview

Pulsatile Secretion

GH is released in bursts, primarily during deep sleep, with additional pulses after exercise and stress.

IGF-1 Mediation

Most GH actions are mediated through insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1) produced mainly in the liver.

Lifespan Pattern

GH secretion peaks during puberty, declines with age, and shows gender differences in secretory patterns.

GH Actions on Metabolism

Anti-Insulin Effects

GH opposes insulin action, increasing hepatic glucose output and reducing peripheral glucose uptake.

Lipolysis Stimulation

GH promotes breakdown of triglycerides in adipose tissue, increasing free fatty acid availability.

Protein Anabolism

GH stimulates amino acid uptake and protein synthesis in muscle and other tissues.

GH Deficiency & Excess

Childhood Deficiency

Leads to growth failure, delayed bone age, and proportionate short stature.

Adult Deficiency

Causes reduced lean mass, increased fat mass, dyslipidemia, and diminished quality of life.

Gigantism

GH excess before epiphyseal closure causes excessive linear growth.

Acromegaly

GH excess after epiphyseal closure causes bony overgrowth of hands, feet, and face, along with soft tissue enlargement.

Factors Boosting GH Secretion

Sleep (especially deep sleep stages), fasting, exercise, stress, and sex steroids (particularly androgens and estrogen) all stimulate GH release.

Prolactin Physiology

Dopamine Brake

Dopamine is the primary inhibitor of prolactin secretion, acting through D2 receptors on lactotropes.

Pregnancy Priming

Estrogen increases lactotrope number and sensitivity to stimulatory factors during pregnancy.

Suckling Reflex

Nipple stimulation inhibits dopamine release and stimulates prolactin secretion to maintain lactation.

Prolactin Inhibits Fertility

GnRH Suppression

Elevated prolactin inhibits GnRH pulsatility, reducing gonadotropin secretion and gonadal function.

Lactational Amenorrhea

High prolactin during breastfeeding suppresses ovulation, providing natural contraception.

Prolactinoma Clinical Note

Epidemiology

Prolactinomas are the most common functional pituitary adenomas, more frequent in women than men.

Symptoms

Galactorrhea, menstrual irregularities, infertility, and in large tumors, mass effects like visual field defects.

First-Line Therapy

Dopamine agonists (bromocriptine, cabergoline) effectively shrink tumors and normalize prolactin levels.

Posterior Pituitary

ADH and Oxytocin Origins

Hypothalamic Synthesis

Both ADH (vasopressin) and oxytocin are synthesized in the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus.

Axonal Transport

These hormones are transported down axons through the pituitary stalk to nerve terminals in the posterior pituitary.

On-Demand Release

Release occurs directly into capillaries in response to specific physiological stimuli.

ADH Controls Osmolality

Osmoreceptor Sensitivity

Hypothalamic osmoreceptors detect small changes in plasma osmolality (as little as 1-2%), triggering ADH release.

V2 Receptor Action

ADH binds V2 receptors in renal collecting ducts, inserting aquaporin-2 water channels to increase water reabsorption.

Volume Backup

Significant hypovolemia can stimulate ADH release even without increased osmolality.

Diabetes Insipidus Alert

Central DI

Deficient ADH production due to hypothalamic or pituitary damage, treatable with desmopressin.

Nephrogenic DI

Renal resistance to ADH action, often due to medications, electrolyte disorders, or genetic defects.

Water Deprivation Test

Diagnostic test to distinguish between central and nephrogenic DI and primary polydipsia.

Management Pearls

Central DI is managed with desmopressin replacement. Nephrogenic DI may respond to thiazides, NSAIDs, or amiloride. Ensuring free access to water is critical in both forms.

Therapeutic Oxytocin

Labor Indication

Used to induce or augment labor by stimulating uterine contractions.

Hemorrhage Control

Administered postpartum to prevent or treat uterine atony and hemorrhage.

Safety Limits

Requires careful titration to avoid hyperstimulation, fetal distress, or uterine rupture.

Thyroid Gland

Thyroid Embryology and Anatomy

Pharyngeal Floor Origin

The thyroid descends from the foramen cecum at the base of the tongue, migrating caudally while connected by the thyroglossal duct. Remnants of this migration pathway can form midline cysts.

Rich Vascular Supply

Fenestrated perifollicular capillaries deliver high blood flow per gram, ensuring rapid iodide uptake and hormone delivery to systemic circulation.

Follicular Architecture

The thyroid is composed of spherical follicles lined by follicular cells and filled with colloid containing thyroglobulin.

Colloid Storage Design

Luminal thyroglobulin acts as an extracellular hormone reservoir, unique among endocrine glands. This allows weeks of hormone supply despite dietary iodine fluctuations.

TSH-Driven Remodeling

Chronic TSH overstimulation enlarges follicles, producing goiter visible on neck exam, a hallmark of iodine deficiency or thyroid resistance.

Iodine Handling Pathway

Active Iodide Trapping

The basolateral Na+/I- symporter (NIS) concentrates plasma iodide 20-40 fold, driven by sodium gradient, forming the first committed step in hormone synthesis.

Apical Pendrin Transport

Pendrin moves iodide into the follicular lumen where thyroperoxidase oxidizes it, enabling subsequent tyrosyl iodination within thyroglobulin.

Recycling Conserves Supply

Peripheral deiodinases liberate iodide from degraded hormones, returning it to the thyroid and minimizing urinary loss during scarcity.

Thyroglobulin Iodination

Coupling Reactions Build T3/T4

Thyroperoxidase links iodinated tyrosines within thyroglobulin, forming T3 and T4 residues that remain stored until TSH-stimulated endocytosis.

Proteolysis Releases Hormones

Lysosomal degradation of endocytosed thyroglobulin liberates free T4 and T3, which exit basally into circulation, completing the synthetic cycle.

T3 and T4 Structure Activity

T4 as Pro-hormone

With a seven-day half-life, T4 serves as a circulating reservoir, converted intracellularly to active T3 by 5'-deiodinases, ensuring tissue-specific control.

rT3 Inactivates Signal

Inner-ring deiodination produces reverse T3, which binds receptors without activation, providing a rapid off-switch during illness or starvation.

Thyroid Receptor Mechanisms

TRalpha and TRbeta Isoforms

These nuclear receptors heterodimerize with RXR, binding thyroid response elements to activate genes for mitochondrial uncoupling, myosin heavy chains, and NaK-ATPase.

Unliganded Repression

In the absence of T3, TR recruits corepressors, silencing transcription; hormone binding swaps these for coactivators, flipping the genomic switch.

Metabolic Actions of Thyroid

Raising Basal Metabolic Rate

T3 stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis and futile cycles, increasing oxygen consumption and heat production, evident as warm skin and heat intolerance in hyperthyroidism.

Synergy with Catecholamines

Thyroid hormone up-regulates beta-adrenergic receptors, amplifying epinephrine effects, explaining tachycardia and tremor in hyperthyroid patients.

Lipid and Carbohydrate Turnover

It accelerates cholesterol clearance and glycogenolysis, providing substrates for the heightened metabolic demands of a hyperthyroid state.

Developmental Actions

Fetal Brain Maturation

T3 regulates neuronal migration, synaptogenesis, and myelination; deficiency during gestation causes irreversible intellectual disability and deafness.

Skeletal Growth Coordination

Thyroid hormone synergizes with GH to promote chondrocyte differentiation and ossification, ensuring timely bone age advancement.

Thyroid Autoimmunity

Hashimoto Destruction

Anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies mediate lymphocytic infiltration, leading to apoptotic follicular loss and hypothyroidism.

Graves Stimulation

TSI antibodies mimic TSH, causing diffuse hyperplasia and hyperthyroidism, often accompanied by ophthalmopathy through cross-reactive orbital antigens.

Iodine Deficiency Disorders

Goiter and Hypothyroidism

Low dietary iodide impairs hormone synthesis, elevating TSH and triggering follicular hypertrophy visible as neck swelling and fatigue.

Cretinism Prevention

Universal salt iodization programs have nearly eliminated the severe neurodevelopmental syndrome of deaf-mutism and intellectual disability.

Thyroid Hormone Synthesis Steps

Iodide Trapping

Basolateral Na+/I- symporter (NIS) concentrates plasma iodide 30- to 40-fold against electrochemical gradient, driven by Na+-K+ ATPase.

Oxidation & Organification

Thyroperoxidase oxidizes iodide to reactive iodine species that bind to tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin.

Coupling Reaction

Iodinated tyrosines couple to form T3 and T4 within the thyroglobulin molecule.

Endocytosis & Release

Thyroglobulin is endocytosed and proteolyzed to release T4 and T3 into circulation.

T4 to T3 Conversion

Peripheral Activation

5'-deiodinases in liver, kidney, and other tissues convert T4 to active T3, providing tissue-specific control of thyroid hormone action.

Sick Euthyroid

Illness inhibits deiodinase activity, reducing T3 production as an adaptive response to conserve energy.

Drug Inhibition

Several medications including propylthiouracil, amiodarone, and beta-blockers can inhibit T4 to T3 conversion.

Thyroid Transport Proteins

TBG Dominance

Thyroxine-binding globulin carries about 70% of circulating thyroid hormones, with transthyretin and albumin carrying the remainder.

Estrogen Effect

Estrogen increases TBG synthesis, raising total thyroid hormone levels while free levels remain normal.

Acute Illness

Critical illness can reduce TBG levels and inhibit binding, altering total thyroid hormone measurements.

Regulation Axis TRH-TSH-T3

TRH Pulse Generator

Hypothalamic TRH neurons fire in pulses that entrain TSH secretion from the pituitary.

TSH Glycosylation

Proper glycosylation is essential for TSH bioactivity, with alterations occurring in various thyroid disorders.

Negative Feedback

T3 suppresses TRH and TSH synthesis and secretion, maintaining thyroid hormone levels within narrow limits.

Graves Disease Mechanism

Autoantibody Mimicry

TSI antibodies activate the TSH receptor, causing unregulated thyroid hormone production.

Suppressed TSH

Elevated thyroid hormones suppress TSH secretion, distinguishing Graves disease from TSH-secreting tumors.

Extrathyroidal Manifestations

Orbital fibroblasts express TSH receptor, explaining the ophthalmopathy that often accompanies Graves disease.

Treatment Trio

Graves disease can be managed with antithyroid drugs (methimazole, propylthiouracil), radioactive iodine ablation, or thyroidectomy, each with specific indications and risks.

Systemic Actions of Thyroid Hormones

Calorigenesis

Increases basal metabolic rate and thermogenesis.

Cardiovascular

Enhances cardiac output through increased heart rate, contractility, and blood volume.

Skeletal Maturation

Essential for normal bone development and linear growth in children.

Neurological

Critical for CNS development and maintenance of normal cognitive function in adults.

Hypothyroid vs Hyperthyroid

Metabolic Rate

Hypothyroidism: decreased BMR, cold intolerance. Hyperthyroidism: increased BMR, heat intolerance.

Cardiovascular

Hypothyroidism: bradycardia, decreased cardiac output. Hyperthyroidism: tachycardia, increased cardiac output.

Neuropsychiatric

Hypothyroidism: depression, cognitive slowing. Hyperthyroidism: anxiety, irritability.

Gastrointestinal

Hypothyroidism: constipation, decreased motility. Hyperthyroidism: diarrhea, increased motility.

Adrenal Cortex

Adrenal Zones & Products

Zona Glomerulosa

Outermost layer producing mineralocorticoids (mainly aldosterone) under control of renin-angiotensin system.

Zona Fasciculata

Middle layer producing glucocorticoids (mainly cortisol) under ACTH control.

Zona Reticularis

Innermost layer producing adrenal androgens (DHEA, androstenedione) under ACTH control.

Cholesterol to Cortisol Pathway

Rate-Limiting Step

Cholesterol side-chain cleavage by P450scc converts cholesterol to pregnenolone, the committed step in steroidogenesis.

17α-Hydroxylase

Key enzyme directing steroid synthesis toward cortisol and androgens rather than aldosterone.

21 & 11β Hydroxylations

Essential steps in both cortisol and aldosterone synthesis pathways.

Enzyme Deficiency Consequences

Deficiencies in steroidogenic enzymes cause congenital adrenal hyperplasia, with specific patterns of hormone excess and deficiency depending on the blocked step.

Aldosterone Regulation

RAAS Cascade

Renin release from juxtaglomerular cells initiates angiotensin II formation, the primary stimulator of aldosterone secretion.

Potassium Signal

Hyperkalemia directly stimulates aldosterone production independent of angiotensin II.

ACTH Minor Role

ACTH can transiently increase aldosterone but is not the major regulator under normal conditions.

Cortisol Negative Feedback

Hypothalamic CRH Inhibition

Cortisol suppresses CRH synthesis and release from paraventricular nucleus.

Pituitary ACTH Suppression

Cortisol directly inhibits ACTH synthesis and secretion from corticotropes.

Clinical Implication

Exogenous glucocorticoids can suppress the HPA axis, requiring gradual tapering to avoid adrenal insufficiency.

Cortisol Metabolic Actions

Hepatic Gluconeogenesis

Stimulates glucose production from amino acids and other precursors.

Protein Catabolism

Promotes breakdown of muscle protein to provide substrates for gluconeogenesis.

Lipolysis Facilitation

Enhances lipid mobilization from adipose tissue.

Insulin Antagonism

Counters insulin action, contributing to hyperglycemia in stress states.

Anti-Inflammatory Cortisol Effects

Cytokine Transcription Block

Inhibits NF-κB and other transcription factors that drive inflammatory gene expression.

Vascular Permeability

Reduces capillary leak and edema formation at sites of inflammation.

Leukocyte Redistribution

Alters trafficking of immune cells, decreasing numbers in circulation while increasing margination.

Clinical Use

These effects underlie the therapeutic use of glucocorticoids in inflammatory and autoimmune conditions.

Adrenal Androgens Role

DHEA Secretion

Dehydroepiandrosterone is the major adrenal androgen, serving as a precursor for more potent androgens and estrogens.

Female Physiology

Adrenal androgens contribute significantly to female androgen levels and are important for libido and pubic/axillary hair.

Male Insignificance

Testicular testosterone production far exceeds adrenal androgen contribution in males.

Cushing Syndrome Features

Central Obesity

Characteristic fat redistribution with increased abdominal, facial (moon facies), and supraclavicular fat.

Skin & Connective Tissue

Thin skin, purple striae, easy bruising, and impaired wound healing.

Muscle & Bone

Proximal muscle weakness and osteoporosis with increased fracture risk.

Metabolic & Immune

Glucose intolerance, hypertension, and increased susceptibility to infections.

Addison Disease Pathology

Autoimmune Destruction

Most common cause in developed countries, with antibodies against 21-hydroxylase.

Cortisol Deficiency

Leads to fatigue, weight loss, hypoglycemia, and inability to handle stress.

Aldosterone Deficiency

Causes salt wasting, hyperkalemia, and hypotension.

Hyperpigmentation

Due to increased ACTH stimulating melanocytes, particularly in sun-exposed areas and skin creases.

Clinical Correlation Quiz

Iatrogenic vs Endogenous

Exogenous glucocorticoid use is the most common cause of Cushing syndrome, while endogenous causes include pituitary adenomas, adrenal tumors, and ectopic ACTH production.

Addison Electrolytes

Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and metabolic acidosis characterize Addison disease due to mineralocorticoid deficiency.

ACTH Rise Mechanism

Loss of cortisol negative feedback in Addison disease causes marked elevation of ACTH.

Skin Manifestations

Hyperpigmentation distinguishes primary from secondary adrenal insufficiency.

Adrenal Medulla

Catecholamine Synthesis Route

Tyrosine Hydroxylase

Rate-limiting enzyme converting tyrosine to L-DOPA.

DOPA Decarboxylase

Converts L-DOPA to dopamine.

PNMT Switch

Phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase converts norepinephrine to epinephrine, exclusively in adrenal medulla.

Storage & Release

Catecholamines are stored in chromaffin granules with ATP and chromogranins, released by exocytosis in response to sympathetic stimulation.

Chromaffin Cell Anatomy

Neuroendocrine Identity

Modified postganglionic sympathetic neurons that secrete directly into blood rather than at synapses.

Blood Supply

Receives rich arterial supply with drainage directly into adrenal veins.

Innervation

Directly innervated by preganglionic sympathetic fibers (ACh, nicotinic receptors).

Epinephrine Fight Effects

β1 Cardiac

Increases heart rate, contractility, and conduction velocity.

β2 Skeletal Vasodilation

Dilates blood vessels to skeletal muscle, enhancing oxygen and nutrient delivery.

α1 Splanchnic Vasoconstriction

Constricts visceral blood vessels, redirecting blood flow to critical organs.

Bronchodilation

Relaxes bronchial smooth muscle, improving airway conductance.

Metabolic Impact of Catecholamines

Hepatic Glycogenolysis

Rapidly increases blood glucose through breakdown of liver glycogen.

Adipose Lipolysis

Stimulates triglyceride breakdown, increasing free fatty acids for energy.

Thermogenesis

Increases metabolic rate and heat production.

Insulin Suppression

Inhibits insulin secretion, favoring glucose availability for brain and muscle.

Pheochromocytoma Pearl

Rule of 10s

Approximately 10% are bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% familial, and 10% occur in children.

Classic Triad

Headache, sweating, and tachycardia are the characteristic symptoms.

Biochemical Diagnosis

Elevated plasma free metanephrines or 24-hour urinary fractionated metanephrines.

Pre-Op Blockade

Alpha-blockade must precede beta-blockade to prevent unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction.

Recall: Medulla vs Cortex

Embryology

Medulla derives from neural crest (ectoderm), cortex from mesoderm.

Stimulus

Medulla responds to neural input (sympathetic), cortex to hormonal signals (ACTH, angiotensin II).

Blood Drainage

Medullary veins drain through cortex, exposing cortical cells to high catecholamine concentrations.

Clinical Disorders

Medulla: pheochromocytoma; Cortex: Cushing syndrome, Addison disease, hyperaldosteronism.

Parathyroid & Calcium Homeostasis

Calcium Distribution in Plasma

Ionized Fraction

Approximately 50% of total calcium is ionized and biologically active, tightly regulated between 1.1–1.3 mmol/L and immediately sensed by calcium-sensing receptors.

Protein Bound

About 40% bound to albumin, influenced by pH and albumin concentration.

Complexed Fraction

Remaining 10% complexed with anions like citrate, phosphate, and bicarbonate.

PTH Secretion Triggers

Calcium-Sensing Receptor

Parathyroid chief cells express CaSR that inversely regulates PTH secretion - high calcium inhibits, low calcium stimulates.

Magnesium Influence

Severe hypomagnesemia can impair PTH secretion despite hypocalcemia.

Catecholamine Potentiation

Beta-adrenergic stimulation can increase PTH secretion, particularly in hyperparathyroidism.

PTH Bone and Kidney Actions

Osteoblast Signaling

PTH binds osteoblast receptors, stimulating RANKL production that activates osteoclasts to resorb bone.

Distal Tubule Calcium Reabsorption

Enhances renal calcium conservation by increasing reabsorption in the distal nephron.

Phosphate Wasting

Reduces proximal tubular phosphate reabsorption, promoting phosphaturia.

1α-Hydroxylase Stimulation

Increases renal conversion of 25-OH vitamin D to active 1,25-(OH)2 vitamin D.

Vitamin D Activation Cascade

Skin Production

UVB radiation converts 7-dehydrocholesterol to previtamin D3 in skin.

Hepatic 25-Hydroxylation

Vitamin D is converted to 25-OH vitamin D in liver, the major circulating form.

Renal 1α-Hydroxylation

Final activation to 1,25-(OH)2 vitamin D occurs primarily in proximal tubule under PTH control.

Vitamin D Intestinal Effect

Genomic Up-regulation

Increases transcription of calcium and phosphate transport proteins in enterocytes.

Dual Mineral Absorption

Enhances both calcium and phosphate absorption from diet.

Negative Feedback

1,25-(OH)2 vitamin D suppresses its own production by inhibiting 1α-hydroxylase.

Calcitonin Minor Role

Secretion Trigger

Released from thyroid parafollicular C-cells in response to hypercalcemia.

Physiologic Impact

Weakly inhibits osteoclast activity and promotes renal calcium excretion, but not essential for calcium homeostasis.

Hypocalcemia Signs

Neuromuscular Irritability

Increased nerve excitability due to reduced calcium stabilization of voltage-gated sodium channels.

Chvostek Sign

Facial muscle twitching upon tapping facial nerve.

Carpopedal spasm with blood pressure cuff inflation.

Emergency Tetany

Severe hypocalcemia can cause laryngospasm, seizures, and cardiac arrhythmias.

Respiratory Alkalosis

Increases calcium binding to albumin, reducing ionized calcium and potentially causing symptoms.

PTH Phosphate Effect

PTH decreases renal phosphate reabsorption, lowering serum phosphate.

1α-Hydroxylase Enzyme

Key enzyme in vitamin D activation, stimulated by PTH and inhibited by hyperphosphatemia.

Calcimimetic Drug

Endocrine Pancreas

Islet Cell Architecture

Core-Shell Design

Beta cells form the central core surrounded by alpha, delta, and PP cells in the periphery, facilitating paracrine interactions.

Fenestrated Capillaries

Rich capillary network with fenestrations allows rapid hormone entry into circulation.

Intra-islet Innervation

Autonomic nerves modulate hormone secretion in response to feeding status and stress.

Insulin Synthesis Journey

Gene Transcription

INS gene on chromosome 11 transcribed to preproinsulin mRNA.

Preproinsulin Cleavage

Signal peptide removed in ER to form proinsulin.

Golgi Maturation

Proinsulin packaged into secretory granules where proteases cleave it to insulin and C-peptide.

Secretory Granule Storage

Mature insulin stored in granules awaiting secretory signals.

Glucose Triggers Insulin

GLUT-2 Transport

Beta cells express GLUT-2 transporters that allow rapid glucose entry proportional to blood concentration.

ATP Generation

Glucose metabolism increases ATP:ADP ratio, closing ATP-sensitive potassium channels.

Ca2+ Influx

Membrane depolarization opens voltage-gated calcium channels, triggering exocytosis of insulin granules.

Biphasic Secretion

Rapid first phase from readily releasable granules, sustained second phase from reserve pool mobilization.

Insulin Receptor Cascade

Receptor Structure

Tetrameric receptor with extracellular alpha subunits and transmembrane beta subunits with tyrosine kinase activity.

IRS-PI3K-Akt Pathway

Primary metabolic signaling pathway promoting GLUT-4 translocation, glycogen synthesis, and protein synthesis.

MAPK Branch

Alternate pathway stimulating cell growth and differentiation.

Insulin Lowers Glucose

GLUT-4 Translocation

Recruits glucose transporters to muscle and fat cell membranes, increasing glucose uptake.

Glycogenesis

Stimulates glycogen synthesis in liver and muscle.

Gluconeogenesis Inhibition

Suppresses hepatic glucose production.

Glycolysis Activation

Enhances glucose utilization in tissues.

Insulin Anabolic Actions

Protein Synthesis

Stimulates amino acid uptake and ribosomal translation.

Lipogenesis

Promotes fatty acid and triglyceride synthesis in liver and adipose tissue.

Potassium Uptake

Stimulates Na+/K+ ATPase, lowering serum potassium.

Glucagon Opposes Insulin

Hepatic Receptor

Binds G-protein-coupled receptors on hepatocytes, activating cAMP-PKA pathway.

Glycogenolysis

Stimulates breakdown of liver glycogen to glucose.

Glycogen Synthase Inhibition

Prevents glycogen synthesis during fasting state.

Glucagon Gluconeogenesis Boost

Gene Transcription

Increases expression of PEPCK and other gluconeogenic enzymes.

Allosteric Control

Activates key enzymes while inhibiting glycolytic enzymes.

Substrate Supply

Promotes hepatic uptake of amino acids and glycerol for gluconeogenesis.

Somatostatin Brake

δ-Cell Secretion

Released from pancreatic delta cells in response to nutrients and other hormones.

Paracrine Inhibition

Locally suppresses both insulin and glucagon secretion.

Physiologic Role

Modulates islet hormone secretion to prevent extreme glucose fluctuations.

Type 1 Diabetes Pathology

Autoimmune Destruction

T-cell mediated attack on beta cells, with autoantibodies against GAD, IA-2, and insulin.

Absolute Insulin Deficiency

Progressive loss of beta cell mass leads to complete insulin dependence.

Loss of Glucagon Brake

Insulin deficiency removes normal suppression of glucagon, contributing to hyperglycemia.

DKA Mechanism

Absolute insulin deficiency with counterregulatory hormone excess causes uncontrolled lipolysis, ketogenesis, and metabolic acidosis.

Type 2 Diabetes Progression

Insulin Resistance

Reduced tissue sensitivity to insulin, initially compensated by hyperinsulinemia.

β-Cell Compensation

Beta cells increase insulin secretion to maintain normoglycemia.

Relative Deficiency

Progressive beta cell failure leads to insufficient insulin for metabolic demands.

Therapeutic Trajectory

Treatment evolves from lifestyle and oral agents to injectable medications including insulin as beta cell function declines.

C-Peptide Clinical Value

β-Cell Function Marker

Measurement reflects endogenous insulin production.

Differential Diagnosis

Helps distinguish type 1 (low C-peptide) from type 2 diabetes (normal/high C-peptide).

Therapeutic Guidance

Indicates residual beta cell function that may influence treatment choices.
