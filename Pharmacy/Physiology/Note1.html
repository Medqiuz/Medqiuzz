Digestive System & Secretory Functions

A Comprehensive Guide to Digestion, Absorption, and Gastrointestinal Secretions

Digestive System Overview

Functions of the Digestive System

  • Reduces particle size of ingested food
  • Absorbs micronutrients and trace elements for bodily functions
  • Sets a physical and immunologic barrier against pathogens
  • Digests macronutrients into absorbable forms:
    • Carbohydrates (starch and sugar) → single sugar molecules
    • Proteins → amino acids
    • Fats → fatty acids, glycerol

Anatomy of the Digestive System

The digestive system extends from the mouth to the anus and includes:

  • Epithelial lining that forms a barrier to invaders
  • Submucosal layer containing glands and blood vessels
  • Muscularis for movement and peristalsis
  • Accessory organs: salivary glands, pancreas, liver, gallbladder

Peristalsis

Muscular contractions that move food along throughout the GI tract. These wave-like contractions propel food forward while mixing digestive secretions with the food mass.

Mouth and Esophagus

The Mouth

Mastication (Chewing)

Mechanical breakdown of food into smaller particles to increase surface area for enzyme action.

Saliva and Salivary Glands

  • Salivary glands produce about 1.5 liters of saliva daily
  • Salivary amylase (ptyalin): begins starch digestion (inactivated in stomach)
  • Salivary lipase: begins fat digestion with minimal effect
  • Mucus: lubricates food for easier swallowing
  • Lysozyme: antibacterial enzyme

Tongue Functions

  • Taste receptors: detect sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami (savory)
  • Enzymes to help break down fatty acids

Swallowing Process

  • Bolus: food mass swallowed at one time
  • Epiglottis blocks windpipe to prevent choking
  • Coordinated muscle movements propel food from mouth to esophagus

Esophageal Functions

  • Long tube connecting pharynx to stomach
  • Peristalsis moves food toward stomach
  • Lower esophageal sphincter prevents backflow of stomach contents
  • Mucus secretion for lubrication
  • Dysfunction can lead to heartburn (GERD)

Stomach

Gastric Anatomy and Control

  • Lower esophageal sphincter and pyloric sphincter control entry and exit
  • Storage capacity of ~4 cups (about 1 liter)
  • Secretes 2000-2500 ml of gastric juice daily

Gastric Secretions

  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl): creates acidic environment (pH 1-4)
  • Pepsinogen: converted to active pepsin for protein digestion
  • Gastric lipase: minor effect on shorter-chain triglycerides
  • Mucus: protective layer
  • Intrinsic factor: essential for vitamin B12 absorption
  • Gastrin: hormone regulating gastric function

Stomach Functions

  • Kills bacteria with highly acidic environment
  • Protein digestion begins with pepsin activation
  • Forms chyme: semi-liquid mixture of food and gastric secretions
  • Limited absorption of some water, ions, alcohol, and certain drugs
  • Regulates emptying into small intestine

Importance of Stomach Acid

  • Activates digestive enzymes (pepsinogen → pepsin)
  • Partially digests dietary protein
  • Assists in calcium absorption
  • Makes dietary minerals soluble for absorption
  • Provides defense against ingested pathogens

Small Intestine

Anatomy and Structure

  • Length: 5-7 meters (16-23 feet)
  • Three sections:
    • Duodenum: 0.5 meters, receives bile and pancreatic secretions
    • Jejunum: 2-3 meters, primary site of nutrient absorption
    • Ileum: 3-4 meters, absorbs vitamin B12 and bile salts

Specialized Absorptive Structures

  • Folded walls with finger-like villi projections
  • Microvilli on absorptive cells create brush border
  • Increases intestinal surface area approximately 600x
  • Rapid cell turnover (every 3-5 days)
  • Contains capillaries and lacteals for nutrient transport

Nutrient Absorption Mechanisms

  • Passive diffusion: driven by concentration gradients (fats, water, some minerals)
  • Facilitated diffusion: carrier-mediated but no energy required
  • Active transport: uses energy against concentration gradient (glucose, amino acids)
  • Phagocytosis and pinocytosis: cell "drinking" of larger molecules (immune substances in breast milk)

Absorption Pathways

  • Water-soluble nutrients → blood → liver → general circulation
  • Fat-soluble nutrients → lymph → thoracic duct → bloodstream
  • Liver detoxifies and repackages absorbed nutrients

Large Intestine

Anatomy and Functions

  • Length: ~3.5 feet (1.5 meters)
  • No villi or digestive enzymes present
  • Little digestion occurs
  • Primary functions:
    • Absorption of water and electrolytes
    • Absorption of some minerals and vitamins
    • Bacterial fermentation of undigested fiber
    • Formation and storage of feces

Bacterial Actions in Colon

  • Break down indigestible fiber
  • Produce gases and organic acids
  • Synthesize vitamins:
    • Vitamin K (essential for blood clotting)
    • Thiamin (B1)
    • Vitamin B12
    • Riboflavin (B2)

Rectum and Elimination

  • Stool storage until elimination
  • Stretch receptors stimulate defecation reflex
  • Muscle contractions propel feces
  • Anal sphincters (internal and external) control elimination

Accessory Organs

Pancreas

Dual Functions

  • Endocrine: produces glucagon and insulin
  • Exocrine: manufactures digestive enzymes

Pancreatic Secretions

  • 1.0-1.2 liters per day
  • Alkaline (pH 7.1-8.2) to neutralize acidic chyme
  • Contains bicarbonate and digestive enzymes:
    • Proteases: trypsin, chymotrypsin, carboxypeptidase (for proteins)
    • Amylase: for carbohydrate digestion
    • Lipase: for fat digestion
    • Nucleases: for nucleic acid digestion

Liver

Major Functions

  • Vascular: blood storage and filtration
  • Metabolic: carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism
  • Storage: glycogen, vitamins, iron
  • Detoxification: drugs and hormones
  • Synthetic: plasma proteins, clotting factors
  • Excretory: bile production

Bile Production and Function

  • Liver produces 600-1200 ml of bile daily
  • Alkaline (pH 7.8-8.6)
  • Composition:
    • Water and electrolytes (97.5%)
    • Bile salts, bile pigments, cholesterol, lecithin (2.5%)
  • Functions:
    • Emulsification of fats for digestion
    • Absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)
    • Excretion of waste products (bilirubin, cholesterol)
    • Neutralization of gastric acid

Gallbladder

  • Stores and concentrates bile between meals
  • Releases bile in response to fatty meals (stimulated by CCK)
  • Concentrates bile 5-10 times by absorbing water
  • Secretes mucus to protect gallbladder wall

Secretory Functions of GIT

Types of Glands

  • Goblet cells: single mucus secretion cells
  • Crypts of Lieberkühn: specialized glands in intestinal pits
  • Oxyntic glands: deep tubular glands in stomach
  • Complex glands: salivary glands, pancreas, liver

Regulation of Glandular Secretion

  • Mechanical stimulation: presence of food
  • Direct contact with food particles
  • Chemical irritation
  • Distention of gut wall
  • Autonomic nervous system:
    • Parasympathetic stimulation: generally increases secretion
    • Sympathetic stimulation: generally decreases secretion
  • Hormonal regulation by gastrointestinal hormones

Importance of Mucus

  • Composition: water, electrolytes, glycoproteins
  • Functions:
    • Excellent lubricant for food passage
    • Protective coating for GI mucosa
    • Adherent qualities for forming feces
    • Resistant to digestive enzymes
    • Buffering capacity for acids and alkalis
    • Contains bicarbonate to neutralize acids

Enzymes and Digestion

Enzyme Overview

Enzymes are proteins that break chemical bonds in food molecules, facilitating digestion and absorption.

Hormonal Regulation of GI Function

Major Gastrointestinal Hormones

  • Gastrin:
    • Source: Gastric mucosa and duodenum
    • Stimulus: Presence of peptides and amino acids
    • Actions: Stimulates HCl and pepsinogen secretion; increases gastric motility and LES tone
  • Secretin:
    • Source: Duodenal mucosa
    • Stimulus: Acid in small intestine
    • Actions: Stimulates pancreatic bicarbonate secretion; also stimulates insulin and pancreatic enzymes
  • Cholecystokinin (CCK):
    • Source: Proximal small bowel
    • Stimulus: Peptides, amino acids, fat, HCl
    • Actions: Stimulates pancreatic enzyme release; gallbladder contraction; inhibits gastric emptying
  • Gastric Inhibitory Polypeptide (GIP):
    • Source: Small intestine
    • Stimulus: Glucose and fat
    • Actions: Inhibits gastrin-stimulated gastric acid secretion
  • Motilin:
    • Source: Stomach and intestines
    • Stimulus: Biliary and pancreatic secretions
    • Actions: Promotes gastric emptying and increases GI motility
  • Somatostatin:
    • Source: Stomach, pancreas, upper small intestine
    • Stimulus: Acidity and products of protein/fat digestion
    • Actions: Inhibits release of gastrin, motilin, and pancreatic secretions; decreases GI motility

Clinical Correlations

Digestive Disorders

  • Heartburn/GERD: Lower esophageal sphincter dysfunction
  • Peptic ulcers: Imbalance between protective and aggressive factors in stomach
  • Gallstones: Crystallization of cholesterol or bilirubin in gallbladder
  • Pancreatitis: Inflammation of pancreas, often due to enzyme autodigestion
  • Malabsorption syndromes: Impaired nutrient absorption (e.g., celiac disease, Crohn's)
  • Diarrhea: Excess secretion or reduced absorption of water in intestines

Refeeding After Starvation

GI tract becomes compromised after prolonged starvation. Gradual refeeding allows cellular proliferation to restore normal gastrointestinal architecture and function over several days.

Fat Absorption Process

The major portion of fat digestion occurs in the small intestine through the action of pancreatic lipase. Bile salts emulsify fats, increasing surface area for enzyme action. Absorbed lipids are packaged into chylomicrons for transport via lymphatic system.
