DNA Structure & Replication

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is a macromolecule composed of nucleotides that stores and transmits genetic information in living organisms. It consists of two strands arranged in a double helix structure, with complementary base pairing (A-T and G-C) allowing for accurate replication.

Nucleotide Composition

DNA is a polymer of nucleotides, each consisting of three components:

  • Nitrogenous base - Purines (adenine, guanine) or pyrimidines (cytosine, thymine)
  • Pentose sugar - Deoxyribose (lacks oxygen at 2' carbon compared to ribose)
  • Phosphate group - Esterified to the 5' carbon of the sugar

DNA Replication Process

DNA replication is a semi-conservative process where each strand of the original DNA serves as a template for the synthesis of a new complementary strand, resulting in two DNA molecules each with one original and one new strand.

1 Initiation

Begins at specific origin sites (oriC in prokaryotes, multiple origins in eukaryotes) with the help of initiator proteins (DnaA in E. coli). The DNA double helix is unwound by helicase enzymes, creating replication bubbles with replication forks at each end.

2 Primer Synthesis

RNA primers are synthesized by primase to provide starting points for DNA synthesis. These short RNA sequences (5-10 nucleotides) are complementary to the template DNA and provide a free 3'-OH group for DNA polymerase to begin synthesis.

3 Elongation

DNA polymerase adds nucleotides in the 5' to 3' direction using the template strand. The leading strand is synthesized continuously, while the lagging strand is synthesized discontinuously in short fragments called Okazaki fragments.

4 Proofreading & Repair

DNA polymerase has 3'‚Üí5' exonuclease activity that corrects errors during synthesis. Mismatch repair systems further enhance replication fidelity, reducing errors to approximately one per billion nucleotides.

5 Ligation

DNA ligase joins Okazaki fragments on the lagging strand by catalyzing the formation of phosphodiester bonds. In eukaryotes, telomerase maintains chromosome ends (telomeres) to prevent shortening with each replication cycle.

DNA Structure Details

The DNA double helix has several important structural features:

  • Antiparallel strands: The two strands run in opposite directions (5'‚Üí3' and 3'‚Üí5')
  • Complementary base pairing: Adenine pairs with thymine (2 hydrogen bonds), guanine pairs with cytosine (3 hydrogen bonds)
  • Major and minor grooves: Provide binding sites for proteins that regulate gene expression
  • Supercoiling: DNA can be twisted upon itself to compact its structure, regulated by topoisomerases

Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

The flow of genetic information follows: DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí Protein. This includes:

  • Replication: DNA ‚Üí DNA (copying genetic information)
  • Transcription: DNA ‚Üí RNA (synthesis of RNA from DNA template)
  • Translation: RNA ‚Üí Protein (synthesis of proteins from mRNA code)

Protein Metabolism

Protein Metabolism encompasses all biochemical processes involved in the synthesis (anabolism) and breakdown (catabolism) of proteins and amino acids in living organisms. This includes digestion, absorption, transport, synthesis, and degradation of proteins, as well as the metabolic fate of amino acids.

Protein Digestion and Absorption

Proteins are broken down into amino acids through enzymatic digestion in a multi-step process:

1 Stomach

Pepsin (activated from pepsinogen by HCl) begins protein digestion by hydrolyzing peptide bonds involving aromatic amino acids. Gastric acidity denatures proteins, making them more accessible to enzymatic cleavage.

2 Small Intestine

Pancreatic enzymes (trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidases) continue digestion. Trypsin activates other proteases in a cascade. Brush border enzymes (aminopeptidases, dipeptidases) complete digestion to free amino acids.

3 Absorption

Amino acids are absorbed via specific transporters in enterocytes. Different carrier systems exist for neutral, basic, acidic, and imino acids. Absorption is primarily active and energy-dependent using sodium-coupled symport mechanisms.

Amino Acid Catabolism

Amino acids undergo catabolism in three distinct stages:

Three Stages of Amino Acid Catabolism

  1. Removal of amino group via transamination or deamination
  2. Catabolism of carbon skeleton to metabolic intermediates (pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, TCA cycle intermediates)
  3. Ammonia disposal through the urea cycle

Transamination

Transamination reactions transfer Œ±-amino groups from amino acids to Œ±-ketoglutarate, forming glutamate and the corresponding Œ±-keto acid. These reactions are catalyzed by aminotransferases (transaminases) and require pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) as a cofactor.

Urea Cycle

The urea cycle converts toxic ammonia to less toxic urea for excretion. It occurs primarily in the liver and involves five enzymatic steps, with two occurring in mitochondria and three in the cytosol.

Protein Turnover and Degradation

Proteins are continuously synthesized and degraded through two main pathways:

Pathway Mechanism Function
Ubiquitin-Proteasome Targeted proteins marked by ubiquitin and degraded by 26S proteasome Selective degradation of short-lived and regulatory proteins
Lysosomal Degradation Bulk degradation via autophagy and endocytosis Non-selective degradation, organelle turnover, extracellular protein uptake

Nitrogen Balance

Nitrogen balance reflects the relationship between nitrogen intake (dietary protein) and nitrogen loss (urine, feces, skin):

  • Positive nitrogen balance: Intake > excretion (growth, pregnancy, recovery)
  • Negative nitrogen balance: Excretion > intake (starvation, trauma, illness)
  • Nitrogen equilibrium: Intake = excretion (healthy adults)

Amino Acid Pool and Metabolic Fates

The amino acid pool consists of amino acids from dietary protein and tissue protein breakdown. These amino acids can be used for:

  • Synthesis of new proteins
  • Production of specialized nitrogen-containing compounds (creatine, neurotransmitters, nucleotides)
  • Energy production through oxidation
  • Gluconeogenesis (glucogenic amino acids)
  • Ketone body production (ketogenic amino acids)

Porphyrin Metabolism

Porphyrins are cyclic compounds formed from four pyrrole rings that readily bind metal ions. Heme, the most important metalloporphyrin in humans, consists of protoporphyrin IX coordinated with ferrous iron (Fe¬≤‚Å∫). Heme serves as a prosthetic group in hemoglobin, myoglobin, cytochromes, catalase, and other hemoproteins.

Heme Biosynthesis

Heme synthesis is an eight-step pathway that occurs partly in mitochondria and partly in the cytosol. The process is highly regulated and varies between tissues, with the liver and bone marrow being the primary sites.

1 ALA Formation

Glycine and succinyl-CoA condense to form Œ¥-aminolevulinic acid (ALA), catalyzed by ALA synthase (ALAS). This is the rate-limiting step in the pathway, with ALAS1 regulated by heme feedback inhibition.

2 Porphobilinogen Formation

Two molecules of ALA condense to form porphobilinogen (PBG), catalyzed by ALA dehydratase. This enzyme is highly sensitive to inhibition by heavy metals like lead.

3 Uroporphyrinogen Formation

Four PBG molecules condense to form hydroxymethylbilane, which is then cyclized to uroporphyrinogen III. Deficiencies in these steps cause acute intermittent porphyria and congenital erythropoietic porphyria.

4 Heme Formation

After several decarboxylation and oxidation steps, protoporphyrin IX is formed. Ferrochelatase then inserts ferrous iron into the protoporphyrin ring to form heme.

Heme Degradation

After approximately 120 days in circulation, senescent erythrocytes are phagocytosed by macrophages in the spleen, liver, and bone marrow, leading to heme degradation:

Steps of Heme Degradation

  1. Biliverdin formation: Heme oxygenase cleaves the porphyrin ring, producing biliverdin, carbon monoxide (CO), and releasing iron.
  2. Bilirubin formation: Biliverdin reductase converts green biliverdin to orange-red bilirubin.
  3. Conjugation: In the liver, bilirubin is conjugated with glucuronic acid to form water-soluble bilirubin diglucuronide.
  4. Excretion: Conjugated bilirubin is excreted in bile and converted by gut bacteria to urobilinogen and stercobilin, which gives feces its brown color.

Types of Porphyrins

Different porphyrins vary in their side chains:

  • Uroporphyrin: Contains acetate and propionate side chains
  • Coproporphyrin: Contains methyl and propionate groups
  • Protoporphyrin IX: Contains vinyl, methyl, and propionate groups (precursor to heme)

Regulation of Heme Synthesis

Heme synthesis is tightly regulated through multiple mechanisms:

  • Feedback inhibition: Heme inhibits ALAS1 transcription and enzyme activity
  • Iron regulation: ALAS2 (erythroid-specific) is regulated by iron availability
  • Enzyme induction: Certain drugs and chemicals can induce cytochrome P450 enzymes, increasing heme demand

Porphyrias

Porphyrias are metabolic disorders resulting from deficiencies in heme biosynthesis enzymes, leading to accumulation of porphyrins and their precursors. They are classified based on their primary symptoms:

Major Types of Porphyrias

  • Acute Intermittent Porphyria (AIP): Deficiency of hydroxymethylbilane synthase; presents with neurovisceral attacks, abdominal pain, and psychiatric symptoms.
  • Porphyria Cutanea Tarda (PCT): Deficiency of uroporphyrinogen decarboxylase; most common porphyria; presents with photosensitivity, skin fragility, and hyperpigmentation.
  • Erythropoietic Protoporphyria (EPP): Deficiency of ferrochelatase; presents with immediate painful photosensitivity and potential liver complications.
  • Congenital Erythropoietic Porphyria (CEP): Deficiency of uroporphyrinogen III synthase; severe photosensitivity with mutilating skin lesions and reddish-brown teeth.

DNA Repair Disorders

Defects in DNA repair mechanisms can lead to various genetic disorders characterized by genomic instability and increased cancer risk:

  • Xeroderma Pigmentosum: Defect in nucleotide excision repair; extreme sensitivity to UV light, high skin cancer risk.
  • Ataxia Telangiectasia: Defect in response to DNA double-strand breaks; cerebellar ataxia, telangiectasias, immunodeficiency.
  • Fanconi Anemia: Defect in interstrand crosslink repair; bone marrow failure, congenital abnormalities, cancer predisposition.

Protein Metabolism Disorders

Various disorders can affect protein metabolism beyond amino acid metabolism:

  • Protein-energy malnutrition: Kwashiorkor (protein deficiency) and marasmus (total calorie deficiency)
  • Urea cycle disorders: Various enzyme deficiencies leading to hyperammonemia and neurological symptoms
  • Protein-losing enteropathies: Conditions causing excessive protein loss through the gastrointestinal tract
