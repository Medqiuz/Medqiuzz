Giardia lamblia - Medical Protozoology Examination

Conceptual

1. Giardia lamblia belongs to which subphylum of protozoa?
Giardia lamblia is a flagellate belonging to the subphylum Mastigophora within the phylum Sarcomastigophora.

Contextual

2. Who first discovered Giardia lamblia and in what year?
Giardia lamblia was first discovered by Antonie van Leeuwenhoek in 1681 when he examined his own diarrheic stool.

Conceptual

3. Which age group shows the highest prevalence of Giardia lamblia infection?
Giardia infection shows higher prevalence in young, malnourished children, particularly in developing countries.

Contextual

4. A survey in Ethiopia showed what prevalence of giardiasis among school children?
In Ethiopia, a countrywide survey showed 8.9% prevalence among school children and 3.1% among residents.

Conceptual

5. What is the primary habitat of Giardia lamblia in the human body?
Giardia lamblia resides in the small intestine, specifically the duodenum and jejunum, where it attaches to the intestinal mucosa.

Conceptual

6. What is the characteristic shape of Giardia lamblia trophozoites?
Giardia trophozoites are characteristically pear-shaped and dorsoventrally flattened, giving them a distinctive appearance.

Contextual

7. How many nuclei are present in a Giardia lamblia trophozoite, and what is their arrangement?
Giardia trophozoites have two nuclei that give them a characteristic "face-like" or "owl-eyed" appearance under the microscope.

Conceptual

8. Which structure does Giardia use for attachment to the intestinal mucosa?
Giardia possesses a ventral sucking disc, a specialized structure for attachment to the intestinal epithelium without tissue invasion.

Contextual

9. How many pairs of flagella does Giardia lamblia trophozoite have?
Giardia trophozoites have four pairs of flagella: anterior, posterior, ventral, and caudal flagella.

Conceptual

10. How many nuclei are found in a mature Giardia lamblia cyst?
Mature Giardia cysts contain four nuclei, though immature cysts may have two nuclei.

Conceptual

11. What is the infective stage of Giardia lamblia?
The mature cyst (tetranucleated) is the infective stage that is transmitted via the fecal-oral route.

Contextual

12. A hiker develops giardiasis after drinking from a mountain stream. What is the most likely route of transmission?
Giardia is commonly transmitted through ingestion of cysts in fecally contaminated water, often from streams or untreated water sources.

Conceptual

13. Which of the following is NOT a recognized mode of Giardia transmission?
Giardia is not vector-borne. It is transmitted through fecal-oral route, person-to-person contact, and oral-anal sexual practices.

Contextual

14. Where does excystation of Giardia cysts occur in the human body?
Excystation occurs in the duodenum, triggered by acidic pH and pancreatic enzymes, releasing trophozoites from cysts.

Conceptual

15. How many trophozoites are produced from each Giardia cyst during excystation?
Each mature Giardia cyst produces two trophozoites during excystation in the duodenum.

Conceptual

16. Does Giardia lamblia invade host tissues?
Giardia does not invade host tissues; it attaches to the intestinal mucosa using its ventral sucking disc without tissue penetration.

Contextual

17. What is the primary pathological mechanism of Giardia-induced diarrhea?
Giardia causes villous blunting, reduced brush-border enzymes, and malabsorption leading to osmotic diarrhea.

Conceptual

18. What is "steatorrhea" in the context of giardiasis?
Steatorrhea is the presence of excess fat in stool, causing greasy, foul-smelling stools, characteristic of chronic giardiasis due to fat malabsorption.

Contextual

19. Which of the following best describes the effect of Giardia on intestinal villi?
Giardia infection causes villous blunting (shortening) and atrophy, reducing the absorptive surface area of the small intestine.

Conceptual

20. Why is malabsorption particularly severe in giardiasis?
Malabsorption results from mechanical damage to microvilli, reduced brush-border enzymes (disaccharidases), and impaired fat absorption.

Conceptual

21. What is the typical incubation period for acute giardiasis?
The incubation period for giardiasis is typically 1-2 weeks (range 3-25 days) after ingestion of cysts.

Contextual

22. A child presents with recurrent greasy, foul-smelling stools, weight loss, and abdominal bloating. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Chronic giardiasis presents with recurrent diarrhea, steatorrhea (greasy, foul-smelling stools), weight loss, malabsorption, and failure to thrive in children.

Conceptual

23. Which symptom is characteristic of acute giardiasis but less common in chronic cases?
Acute giardiasis often presents with sudden onset of watery diarrhea, while chronic cases feature steatorrhea, weight loss, and malabsorption.

Contextual

24. Which population is most at risk for "failure to thrive" due to giardiasis?
Young children with chronic giardiasis are at particular risk for "failure to thrive" due to chronic malabsorption and nutrient deficiencies.

Conceptual

25. Which vitamin deficiency is most commonly associated with chronic giardiasis?
Chronic giardiasis causes fat malabsorption leading to deficiencies of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K).

Conceptual

26. In what type of stool are Giardia trophozoites most likely found?
Trophozoites are found in diarrheic stools, while cysts are found in formed stools. Both may be found in loose stools.

Contextual

27. What is the most common diagnostic method for giardiasis?
Microscopic examination of stool for trophozoites or cysts is the most common diagnostic method for giardiasis.

Conceptual

28. Why might multiple stool samples be needed to diagnose giardiasis?
Cyst excretion in stool is intermittent, so examination of 3 stool specimens collected on alternate days increases diagnostic sensitivity.

Contextual

29. Which of the following is an alternative diagnostic method when stool microscopy is negative but clinical suspicion remains high?
Antigen detection tests (ELISA or rapid immunoassays) on stool samples have higher sensitivity than microscopy and are useful when microscopy is negative.

Conceptual

30. What is the typical appearance of Giardia trophozoites in wet mount preparations?
Giardia trophozoites exhibit a characteristic "falling leaf" or tumbling motility in fresh wet mount preparations.

Conceptual

31. What is the drug of choice for treating giardiasis?
Metronidazole is the drug of choice for giardiasis. Tinidazole is an alternative with similar efficacy and simpler dosing.

Contextual

32. A hiker is planning a trip to an area with known Giardia contamination. Which water treatment method is most effective?
Boiling water for 1 minute (or 3 minutes at high altitudes) is the most reliable method to kill Giardia cysts. Iodine and filtration are alternatives.

Conceptual

33. How long do iodine tablets need to kill Giardia cysts in water at 20°C?
Iodine tablets kill Giardia cysts in 30 minutes at 15°C and 15 minutes at 20°C when used at recommended concentrations.

Contextual

34. Which of the following is NOT an effective prevention strategy for giardiasis?
Prophylactic antibiotics are not recommended for giardiasis prevention. Prevention focuses on hygiene, water safety, and avoiding risk behaviors.

Conceptual

35. Why is normal chlorination insufficient to kill Giardia cysts in water?
Giardia cysts have thick walls that make them resistant to normal chlorine concentrations used in water treatment. Higher concentrations or longer contact times are needed.

Contextual

36. A daycare center has an outbreak of giardiasis among children. What is the most important control measure?
In daycare outbreaks, intensive handwashing, hygiene education, treatment of infected individuals, and environmental cleaning are key control measures.
