Pharmaceutical Calculations - Study Guide

1. Fundamentals of Measurement and Calculations

Significant Figures

Significant figures are consecutive digits that express the value of a number accurately for a given purpose. The accuracy varies with the number of significant figures.

Rules for determining significant figures:

  1. Digits other than zero are always significant.
  2. A zero between digits is significant.
  3. Final zeros after a decimal point are significant.
  4. Zeros used only to show the location of the decimal point are NOT significant.

Examples:

  • 12.5 has 3 significant figures
  • 0.5 has 1 significant figure
  • 0.0605 has 3 significant figures
  • 102.56 has 5 significant figures

Rules for Rounding

  1. When rounding, retain as many figures as will give only one uncertain figure.
  2. In addition/subtraction of approximate numbers, include only as many decimal places as are in the number with the fewest decimal places.
  3. In multiplication/division of approximate numbers, retain no more significant figures than the number having the fewest significant figures.
  4. When multiplying or dividing an approximate number by an absolute number, round to the significant figures of the approximate number.

Accuracy vs. Precision

Accuracy: Closeness of a measured value to the true value.

Precision (Reproducibility or Repeatability): Degree to which repeated measurements under unchanged conditions show the same results.

Measurement of Volume

Selection of measuring instrument should be based on the level of precision required.

Key Principles:

  • Select the graduate with a capacity equal to or just exceeding the volume to be measured.
  • Measurement of small volumes in large graduates increases error.
  • The narrower the bore/chamber, the lesser the error in reading the meniscus.
  • Conical graduates of less than 25-mL capacities are not recommended for pharmaceutical compounding.

Measurement of Weight

Balance Sensitivity: A balance's ability to detect small changes in weight, expressed as the smallest weight increment that causes a perceptible change.

Sensitivity Requirement (SR): The load that will cause a change of one division on the index plate of the balance.

Class A Prescription Balance:

  • SR of 6 mg or less with no load and with 10g in each pan
  • Should not weigh less than 120 mg of material (to avoid >5% error)
  • Maximum capacity: 120g

Aliquot Method of Weighing and Measuring

Aliquot: A fraction, portion, or part that is contained an exact number of times in another.

Used when a degree of precision is beyond the capacity of the instrument at hand.

1
Preliminary Step: Calculate the smallest quantity that can be weighed with desired precision.
2
Step 1: Select a multiple of the desired quantity that can be weighed with required precision.
3
Step 2: Dilute the multiple quantity with an inert substance.
4
Step 3: Weigh the aliquot portion of the dilution that contains the desired quantity.

Example: Weighing 4 mg of atropine sulfate with 5% accuracy using a balance with SR of 6 mg.

1. Minimum weighable amount: 120 mg (to keep error ≤5%)

2. Select multiple: 30x (4 mg × 30 = 120 mg)

3. Dilute 120 mg drug with diluent (lactose) to prepare mixture

4. Weigh aliquot part (1/30 of mixture) containing 4 mg drug

Density, Specific Gravity, and Specific Volume

Density (d) = Mass / Volume

Usually expressed as g/mL or g/cc. Density of water = 1 g/mL.

Specific Gravity (sp gr) = Weight of substance / Weight of equal volume of water

Water is standard for liquids and solids. Standard temperature: 25°C (except alcohol: 15.56°C).

Specific Volume = Volume of substance / Volume of equal weight of water

Specific gravity and specific volume are reciprocals: sp gr × sp vol = 1

Key Relationships:

  • If sp gr > 1: Substance is heavier than water
  • If sp gr < 1: Substance is lighter than water
  • Weight = Volume × sp gr
  • Volume = Weight / sp gr

Percentage of Error

% Error = (Maximum potential error × 100) / Quantity desired

Example: If the maximum potential error is 4 mg in a total of 100 mg:

% Error = (4 × 100) / 100 = 4%

2. Percentage Calculations

Percentage, Ratio and Proportion

Percentage: Means "by the hundred" or "in a hundred".

Ratio: Expresses relative magnitude of two like quantities.

Proportion: Equality between two ratios.

a : b = c : d → a/b = c/d → a × d = b × c

Example: If 3 tablets contain 975 mg aspirin, how many mg in 12 tablets?

3 tablets : 975 mg = 12 tablets : X mg

3/975 = 12/X → X = (975 × 12) / 3 = 3900 mg

Percentage Preparations

Type Definition Expression Calculation
Weight-in-Volume (w/v) Grams of constituent in 100 mL of solution % w/v Grams = (Volume in mL × %)/100
Volume-in-Volume (v/v) mL of constituent in 100 mL of solution % v/v mL = (Total volume × %)/100
Weight-in-Weight (w/w) Grams of constituent in 100 g of preparation % w/w Grams = (Total weight × %)/100

Default interpretations (when not specified):

  • Solutions/suspensions of solids in liquids: % w/v
  • Solutions of liquids in liquids: % v/v
  • Mixtures of solids/semisolids: % w/w
  • Solutions of gases in liquids: % w/v

Conversions of Concentration

1
% to mg/mL: Multiply percentage strength (as whole number) by 10

Example: 4% (w/v) = 4 × 10 = 40 mg/mL

2
Ratio to mg/mL: Divide ratio strength by 1000

Example: 1:10,000 = 1/10000 = 0.0001 g/mL = 0.1 mg/mL

3
g/L to mg/mL: Convert numerator to mg, divide by mL

Example: 1 g/250 mL = 1000 mg/250 mL = 4 mg/mL

Parts per Million (ppm) and Parts per Billion (ppb)

Used for very dilute solutions:

  • ppm: Parts per 1,000,000 (10⁶)
  • ppb: Parts per 1,000,000,000 (10⁹)

Example: Express 5 ppm of iron in water in percentage strength.

5 ppm = 5 parts in 1,000,000 = 5/1,000,000 = 0.0005%

3. Calculations of Doses and Formulation

Calibration of Droppers

Official medicine dropper delivers water drops weighing 45-55 mg each.

Approximately 20 drops of water = 1 mL (1000 mg ÷ 50 mg/drop ≈ 20 drops).

Important: Different liquids have different surface tensions and flow characteristics, so droppers must be calibrated for each specific liquid.

General Dose Calculations

Number of doses = Total quantity / Size of dose
Size of dose = Total quantity / Number of doses
Total quantity = Number of doses × Size of dose

Example: If dose is 200 mg, how many doses in 10 g?

10 g = 10,000 mg

Number of doses = 10,000 mg ÷ 200 mg = 50 doses

Drug Dosage Based on Body Weight

Dose expressed as specific quantity per unit of patient weight (e.g., mg/kg).

Patient dose = Patient weight × Dose per kg

Example: Usual dose of chlorambucil is 150 mcg/kg. How many mg for 154 lb person?

154 lb ÷ 2.2 = 70 kg

Dose = 70 kg × 150 mcg/kg = 10,500 mcg = 10.5 mg

Drug Dosage Based on Body Surface Area (BSA)

Commonly used for chemotherapy and pediatric patients (except neonates).

Child dose = (Child's BSA / Average adult BSA) × Adult dose

Average adult BSA = 1.73 m²

Example: If adult dose is 100 mg, calculate dose for child with BSA 0.83 m².

Child dose = (0.83 m² / 1.73 m²) × 100 mg = 0.48 × 100 mg = 48 mg

Enlarging and Reducing Formulas

Two-step process:

1
Determine the factor: Factor = Desired quantity / Quantity in formula
2
Multiply each ingredient by the factor

Example: Reduce formula from 1000 mL to 240 mL.

Factor = 240 mL / 1000 mL = 0.24

Multiply each ingredient quantity by 0.24

4. Dilution and Concentration

Relationship Between Strength and Total Quantity

If amount of active ingredient remains constant:

  • Strength is inversely proportional to quantity
  • When quantity increases, percentage strength decreases
  • When quantity decreases, percentage strength increases
Q₁ × C₁ = Q₂ × C₂

Where Q = Quantity, C = Concentration

Dilution and Concentration of Liquids

Example: If 500 mL of 15% v/v solution diluted to 1500 mL, what is new percentage?

Q₁ × C₁ = Q₂ × C₂

500 mL × 15% = 1500 mL × C₂

C₂ = (500 × 15) / 1500 = 5%

Triturations

Dilutions of potent medicinal substances (usually 1:10 w/w mixtures).

Prepared by diluting 1 part drug with 9 parts diluent (usually lactose).

Example: How many grams of 1:10 trituration needed to obtain 25 mg drug?

1:10 trituration contains 1 part drug in 10 parts mixture

For 25 mg drug, need 25 mg × 10 = 250 mg = 0.25 g of trituration

Alligation Medial

Calculates weighted average percentage strength of a mixture.

1
Multiply each component's percentage (as decimal) by its quantity
2
Sum all products
3
Divide by total quantity of mixture
4
Multiply by 100 to get percentage

Example: Mixture of 3000 mL 40% alcohol, 1000 mL 60% alcohol, 1000 mL 70% alcohol.

(0.40×3000) + (0.60×1000) + (0.70×1000) = 1200 + 600 + 700 = 2500

2500 ÷ 5000 = 0.50 = 50% alcohol

Alligation Alternate

Calculates number of parts of components needed to make mixture of desired strength.

1
Place desired percentage in center
2
Place component percentages on left side
3
Subtract diagonally (higher - desired, desired - lower)
4
Results give ratio of components to mix

Example: Mix 95% and 50% alcohol to make 70% alcohol.

95% → 20 parts (70-50)

50% → 25 parts (95-70)

Mix 20 parts 95% alcohol with 25 parts 50% alcohol

Specific Gravity of Mixtures

Alligation medial and alternate can be used for specific gravity problems.

Example: What is sp gr of mixture: 1000 mL syrup (sp gr 1.3), 400 mL glycerin (sp gr 1.25), 1000 mL elixir (sp gr 0.95)?

Total volume = 2400 mL

Using alligation medial:

(1.3×1000) + (1.25×400) + (0.95×1000) = 1300 + 500 + 950 = 2750

2750 ÷ 2400 = 1.146 (sp gr of mixture)

Study Tips

  • Always pay attention to units and convert when necessary
  • For percentage calculations, identify whether it's w/v, v/v, or w/w
  • Remember that specific gravity is unitless (ratio of weights)
  • When using aliquot method, always check that minimum weighable amount is respected
  • Practice with real-world examples to build confidence
  • Double-check calculations, especially for patient doses
